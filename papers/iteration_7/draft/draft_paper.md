# Efficacy of a Single Session of Automated Micro-Sponge Eyelid Margin Debridement Versus Sham Treatment for Symptomatic Blepharitis: A Prospective, Randomized, Placebo-Controlled, Double-Masked Trial

[Author et al.]

[Institution], Department of Ophthalmology, University Hospital

---

## Abstract

**Purpose:** Automated eyelid margin debridement devices have been introduced for in-office management of blepharitis, yet rigorous placebo-controlled evidence of their efficacy is lacking. The present study evaluated the efficacy and safety of a single session of automated micro-sponge eyelid margin debridement compared with sham treatment in patients with symptomatic blepharitis.

**Methods:** This prospective, randomized, placebo-controlled, double-masked trial with crossover design enrolled 42 patients with symptomatic blepharitis (Efron grade >= 2) at a single university hospital eye center. Patients were randomized 1:1 to active debridement or sham treatment. Only pre-crossover data (baseline to 4-week follow-up) were analyzed. The primary outcome was change in the Ocular Surface Disease Index (OSDI). Secondary outcomes included Efron grading, tear break-up time (TBUT), and Schirmer's test II. Between-group comparisons used unpaired t-tests or Mann-Whitney U tests, with Cohen's d or rank-biserial correlation for effect sizes.

**Results:** Twenty patients per group completed the study. No statistically significant between-group differences were observed for OSDI (mean change: -2.40 vs. -4.90; P = 0.548; Cohen's d = 0.19), Efron grading (P = 0.730; rbc = -0.095), TBUT (P = 0.277; rbc = -0.205), or Schirmer's test (P = 0.787; rbc = -0.053). All effect sizes were negligible to small. The sham group showed a significant within-group decrease in Efron grade (P = 0.041). No serious adverse events occurred.

**Conclusions:** A single session of automated micro-sponge eyelid margin debridement did not demonstrate superiority over sham treatment at 4 weeks. Clinicians should not recommend single-session device-based debridement as a standalone blepharitis intervention based on current evidence.

**Keywords:** blepharitis; eyelid debridement; micro-sponge device; ocular surface disease index; randomized controlled trial; sham-controlled

## Introduction

Blepharitis is among the most common conditions in ophthalmic practice, affecting an estimated 37-47% of patients presenting to eye care professionals [REF]. Chronic inflammation of the eyelid margins produces ocular discomfort, tear film instability, and meibomian gland dysfunction, creating a cycle that significantly impairs quality of life [REF]. Standard management centers on daily eyelid hygiene -- warm compresses, lid scrubs, and lubricating drops -- yet adherence is often poor and symptomatic relief incomplete [REF].

Automated in-office eyelid margin debridement devices have been developed to mechanically remove debris, collarettes, and bacterial biofilm from the lid margin using rotating micro-sponge heads [REF]. Although the biological rationale is plausible, the evidence base consists predominantly of uncontrolled observational studies that cannot distinguish genuine treatment effects from placebo responses, natural symptom fluctuation, or co-intervention effects [REF]. Placebo-controlled trials with adequate masking are essential to determine whether the specific mechanical action of these devices provides measurable clinical benefit.

The present study aimed to compare the efficacy and safety of a single session of automated micro-sponge eyelid margin debridement with a rigorously designed, patient-indistinguishable sham procedure in patients with symptomatic blepharitis refractory to conventional lid hygiene.

## Methods

### Study Design and Ethics

This prospective, randomized, placebo-controlled, double-masked trial with crossover design was conducted at a single university hospital eye center. The study was approved by the local ethics committee, conformed to the Declaration of Helsinki, and was retrospectively registered in a national clinical trials register. The crossover component served as a recruitment aid (all participants eventually received active treatment); only pre-crossover data (baseline to 4-week follow-up) were analyzed to avoid carryover effects.

### Participants

Patients were recruited between December 2017 and June 2018. Inclusion criteria were: symptomatic blepharitis graded at least Grade 2 on the Efron grading scale, age > 18 years, decimal visual acuity > 0.5, blepharitis refractory to eyelid margin care for at least 4 weeks, and current use of lubricating drops.

Exclusion criteria comprised routine daily contact lens wear, other eyelid or ocular surface diseases, previous ocular surgery, prior treatment with cyclosporine A or corticosteroids, pregnancy, autoimmune diseases (rheumatologic conjunctivitis, Sjogren's syndrome, endocrine orbitopathy), other immunological diseases, acute infections, trachoma, atopic dermatitis, diabetes mellitus, severe systemic diseases, graft-versus-host disease, and antibiotic use within the preceding 4 weeks.

### Randomization and Blinding

Patients were randomized 1:1 using the "blockrand" package in R, with allocation concealed electronically. Treatments were performed by unmasked ophthalmologists. All diagnostic assessments were conducted by a blinded doctoral fellow. Efron grading was assessed independently by two blinded examiners from standardized photographs. All patients were blinded to treatment assignment.

### Interventions

**Active treatment.** Following topical anesthesia, a disposable cleaning head was placed on the micro-sponge device (one per eye) and cleaning gel applied. The upper and lower eyelids of each eye were treated for 2 minutes, with the practitioner moving the device along the eyelid margin to remove debris.

**Sham treatment.** Identical temporal and spatial conditions were maintained. Following topical anesthesia, a cleaning head was placed on the device and cleaning gel applied. Each eye was treated for 2 minutes; however, the practitioner moved the device along the adjacent row of eyelashes on the eyelid skin rather than the eyelid margin, so that no mechanical cleaning of the lid margin occurred. Under topical anesthesia, patients could not distinguish sham from active treatment.

### Outcome Measures

The primary outcome was change in the Ocular Surface Disease Index (OSDI) from baseline to 4 weeks. Secondary outcomes were: (1) Efron grading scale (0-4), quantifying eyelid margin inflammation severity from standardized photographs assessed by two independent blinded examiners; (2) tear break-up time (TBUT), measured with 2% fluorescein solution, repeated three times per eye; and (3) Schirmer's test II, performed after topical anesthesia (oxybuprocaine 0.4%) with test strips on the lateral lower lid for 10 minutes. Safety assessments included visual acuity measurement and adverse event documentation.

### Sample Size

This was an exploratory study. No prior effect size estimates were available for this intervention, and a formal sample size calculation was not performed.

### Statistical Analysis

Categorical variables were compared using Chi-Square or Fisher's Exact tests as appropriate. Continuous variables were tested for normality (Shapiro-Wilk test). Normally distributed data (OSDI) were compared with paired t-tests (within-group) and unpaired t-tests (between-group). Non-normally distributed data (Efron grading, TBUT, Schirmer's test) were compared with Wilcoxon signed-rank tests (within-group) and Mann-Whitney U tests (between-group). Effect sizes were reported as Cohen's d (thresholds: 0.2 small, 0.5 medium, 0.8 large) or rank-biserial correlation (rbc; thresholds: 0.10 small, 0.30 medium, 0.50 large). Analyses were conducted using R versions 4.3.1 and 4.4.1.

## Results

### Participant Flow

Forty-two patients were enrolled and randomized (21 per group). Two patients were lost to follow-up (one per group), yielding 20 patients per group for analysis.

### Treatment Outcomes

Treatment outcomes are summarized in Table 2. No statistically significant between-group differences were observed for any outcome measure.

**OSDI (primary outcome).** Mean OSDI change was -2.40 points (95% CI: -7.95 to 3.16) in the active group and -4.90 points (95% CI: -11.51 to 1.72) in the sham group. The between-group difference was not significant (P = 0.548; Cohen's d = 0.19). Neither within-group change reached significance (active: P = 0.378; sham: P = 0.138).

**Efron grading.** Mean change was -0.2 (95% CI: -0.56 to 0.16) in the active group and -0.3 (95% CI: -0.57 to -0.03) in the sham group (between-group P = 0.730; rbc = -0.095). The sham group showed a statistically significant within-group decrease (P = 0.041); the active group did not (P = 0.276).

**TBUT.** Mean change was +0.81 s (95% CI: -0.9 to 2.52) in the active group and -0.20 s (95% CI: -1.21 to 0.81) in the sham group (between-group P = 0.277; rbc = -0.205). Neither within-group change was significant (active: P = 0.240; sham: P = 0.808).

**Schirmer's test II.** Mean change was +0.94 mm (95% CI: -1.74 to 3.61) in the active group and +0.95 mm (95% CI: -1.05 to 2.95) in the sham group (between-group P = 0.787; rbc = -0.053). Neither within-group change was significant (active: P = 0.717; sham: P = 0.397).

All effect sizes were negligible to small across outcomes.

### Safety

No significant decrease in visual acuity occurred in either group. In the active group, three patients reported transient eye irritation lasting a few hours post-treatment, and one patient experienced unilateral itching beginning one week post-treatment that persisted until the 4-week follow-up. In the sham group, two patients reported transient eye irritation lasting a few hours, and one patient developed epiphora at the time of treatment. No serious adverse events were recorded.

## Discussion

The present study found that a single session of automated micro-sponge eyelid margin debridement did not demonstrate superiority over sham treatment for any measured outcome in patients with symptomatic blepharitis at 4-week follow-up. All between-group comparisons were non-significant, and effect sizes were uniformly negligible to small (Cohen's d = 0.19 for the primary OSDI outcome; rbc values from -0.053 to -0.205 for secondary outcomes). These results suggest that the specific mechanical cleaning action of the device does not confer measurable short-term benefit beyond that produced by a sham peri-marginal procedure.

Several factors may explain the null findings. A single debridement session may be insufficient to disrupt established lid margin biofilm and alter the chronic inflammatory milieu of blepharitis [REF]. Repeated sessions, as employed in some uncontrolled studies reporting benefit [REF], may be required for cumulative effect. The sham procedure itself -- involving cleaning gel application and device contact with periorbital skin -- may have conferred partial therapeutic benefit, narrowing the between-group contrast. This interpretation is supported by the statistically significant within-group decrease in Efron grading in the sham group (P = 0.041), which likely reflects a combination of placebo effect, partial treatment effect from the gel, and regression to the mean on an ordinal scale with limited granularity. The concurrent use of lubricating drops in all participants may also have elevated the baseline standard of care sufficiently to reduce any incremental benefit of debridement.

The significant baseline OSDI imbalance (sham median 33.3 vs. active median 15.6; P = 0.020) is a notable confound. Higher baseline scores in the sham group create greater potential for regression to the mean, which may partly account for the numerically larger (though non-significant) OSDI improvement in that group. Such imbalances are an inherent risk in small exploratory trials and highlight the need for adequately powered confirmatory studies.

These results align with the broader challenge of evaluating procedural interventions for blepharitis, where placebo effects are substantial and uncontrolled study designs tend to overestimate treatment efficacy [REF]. The present sham-controlled design was specifically engineered to isolate the mechanical debridement component -- the sham was indistinguishable from active treatment under topical anesthesia -- and the null result challenges claims based on uncontrolled data. The finding that the sham group improved on Efron grading while the active group did not further underscores how procedural context alone can produce measurable changes.

### Clinical Implications

These data have direct implications for practice. Based on the present results, clinicians should not recommend single-session automated eyelid margin debridement as a standalone treatment for symptomatic blepharitis. The device did not outperform sham on any subjective or objective endpoint. Patients seeking in-office blepharitis treatments should be informed that placebo-controlled evidence does not support a single debridement session as an effective intervention. Management should continue to prioritize established, guideline-supported approaches: consistent daily lid hygiene with warm compresses and lid scrubs, and topical anti-inflammatory or antibiotic therapy when appropriate [REF]. If debridement devices are considered, they should be used only as part of multi-session protocols or in combination with pharmacological therapies, and patients should be counseled regarding the current evidence limitations.

### Strengths and Limitations

Key strengths include the randomized, double-masked, sham-controlled design with a carefully constructed sham procedure that was indistinguishable to patients under topical anesthesia, independent blinded Efron grading by two examiners, and use of validated outcome measures. Limitations include the small sample size (n = 20 per group) without formal power calculation, raising the possibility of type II error; evaluation of only a single treatment session; a 4-week follow-up that may have been too short to capture delayed effects; the significant baseline OSDI imbalance; single-center design limiting generalizability; and retrospective trial registration.

### Conclusions

A single session of automated micro-sponge eyelid margin debridement did not demonstrate superiority over sham treatment for symptoms (OSDI) or signs (Efron grading, TBUT, Schirmer's test) of blepharitis at 4 weeks. Clinicians should not offer single-session device-based debridement as a standalone blepharitis treatment and should instead rely on established daily lid hygiene and pharmacological therapies as the foundation of care. Adequately powered, multi-session trials with extended follow-up are warranted to determine whether repeated automated debridement confers benefit in chronic blepharitis management.
