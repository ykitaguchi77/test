# Long-term Outcomes of Trabeculectomy Versus Canaloplasty in Open-Angle Glaucoma: An 11-Year Follow-Up of the TVC Study Cohort

---

## Abstract

**Purpose:** To evaluate the long-term surgical outcomes of trabeculectomy (TE) versus canaloplasty (CP) in patients with open-angle glaucoma from the prospective, randomized Trabeculectomy versus Canaloplasty (TVC) study cohort after a mean follow-up exceeding 11 years.

**Methods:** This prospective follow-up study re-examined patients from the original single-center TVC randomized clinical trial conducted at the University Hospital Wuerzburg, Germany (ClinicalTrials.gov: NCT01228799). Primary endpoints were complete success (target intraocular pressure [IOP] without glaucoma medication) and qualified success (target IOP with or without medication), defined as IOP ≤ 18 mmHg (Definition 1) or IOP ≤ 21 mmHg with ≥ 20% reduction from baseline (Definition 2). Secondary endpoints included IOP change, medication use, complications, and revision surgeries. Kaplan-Meier survival analysis with log-rank testing was used for cumulative success probability.

**Results:** Twenty-eight patients (15 TE, 13 CP) were included, with mean follow-up of 11.9 ± 1.1 years (TE) and 11.0 ± 2.0 years (CP). Complete success rates for TE versus CP were 53.3% versus 15.4% (Definition 1, P = 0.06) and 46.7% versus 15.4% (Definition 2, P = 0.22). Qualified success rates were 73.3% versus 69.2% (Definition 1, P = 1.0) and 66.7% versus 76.9% (Definition 2, P = 0.69). Median IOP was significantly lower in TE (10.0 mmHg) than CP (14.0 mmHg; P < 0.01). Hypotony maculopathy occurred in 13.3% of TE eyes and was not observed in CP.

**Conclusions:** After 11 years, trabeculectomy achieved greater IOP reduction and higher complete success rates than canaloplasty, but with a higher complication rate. Canaloplasty remains a reliable and safer alternative for patients with moderately low target pressures who can tolerate adjunctive medication.

---

## Introduction

Glaucoma is a progressive optic neuropathy and a leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide. Elevated intraocular pressure (IOP) remains the most important modifiable risk factor, and surgical intervention is indicated when medical and laser therapies fail to achieve adequate pressure control. Among the surgical options for open-angle glaucoma (OAG), trabeculectomy (TE) and canaloplasty (CP) represent two fundamentally different approaches to aqueous outflow enhancement.

Trabeculectomy, introduced in the late 1960s, creates a guarded fistula allowing aqueous humor to drain subconjunctivally and has long served as the reference standard for incisional glaucoma surgery. Mitomycin C (MMC) is routinely applied during the procedure to modulate wound healing and improve long-term bleb survival. Canaloplasty, by contrast, is a non-penetrating procedure that restores physiological aqueous drainage by dilating Schlemm's canal and placing a tensioning suture to maintain its patency. Because canaloplasty does not create a filtering bleb, it avoids many bleb-related complications but may achieve less pronounced IOP reduction.

The Trabeculectomy versus Canaloplasty (TVC) study, published by Matlach et al. in 2015, was the first prospective, randomized clinical trial to directly compare these two procedures [1]. That initial report demonstrated that trabeculectomy achieved greater IOP reduction and higher complete success rates at 24 months, but at the cost of a higher complication rate. While short- and medium-term data are valuable, long-term follow-up is essential for informed surgical decision-making, as success rates may decline and complication profiles may evolve over time. To date, few studies have reported outcomes beyond five years for either procedure, and no prior randomized comparison extends beyond the medium term.

The present study aimed to evaluate the long-term surgical outcomes of the TVC study cohort after a mean follow-up exceeding 11 years. To the best of our knowledge, this represents the longest follow-up of a randomized comparison between trabeculectomy and canaloplasty reported to date.

## Methods

### Study Design and Setting

This was a prospective follow-up study of the original TVC study cohort. The TVC study was a prospective, single-center, randomized clinical trial conducted at the Department of Ophthalmology, University Hospital Wuerzburg, Germany, comparing the surgical outcomes of trabeculectomy and canaloplasty in patients with open-angle glaucoma (ClinicalTrials.gov Identifier: NCT01228799).

### Participants

The original trial enrolled 62 patients with open-angle glaucoma (including primary open-angle glaucoma [POAG], pigmentary glaucoma [PG], and pseudoexfoliation glaucoma [PEXG]) who were randomized to receive either trabeculectomy (n = 32) or canaloplasty (n = 30) and were followed prospectively for 24 months. Of the 2-year completers (TE n = 31, CP n = 23), all 49 surviving and contactable patients were invited for long-term follow-up. Patients were excluded if death was confirmed via case files or by relatives, if they refused to participate, or if they had an unknown address. Of 49 patients contacted, 28 were included in the final analysis (TE n = 15, CP n = 13). All participants were of Caucasian ethnicity.

### Surgical Techniques

Trabeculectomy was performed with intraoperative application of mitomycin C. Canaloplasty was performed as an ab externo procedure with circumferential dilation of Schlemm's canal and placement of a tensioning suture. In the postoperative management protocol of the original study, antiglaucomatous drugs were added in case of elevated IOP after failed laser goniopuncture in canaloplasty patients and after scleral flap revision or bleb needling in trabeculectomy patients.

### Examination Protocol

Intraocular pressure was measured on two separate occasions using Goldmann applanation tonometry, with a one-hour interval between measurements. One measurement was obtained in miosis and the other after instillation of tropicamide eye drops. Consistent with the original TVC study protocol, no washout of glaucoma medications was performed.

Spectral-domain optical coherence tomography (SD-OCT) was performed using the Heidelberg Spectralis OCT system (Heidelberg Engineering, Heidelberg, Germany). The global retinal nerve fiber layer (RNFL) thickness value (G-value) was recorded. Only scans meeting a quality threshold of Q > 20 were included in the analysis.

Visual field testing was conducted using the Octopus 900 static perimeter (Haag-Streit, Koeniz, Switzerland) with the 30-2 test program. Mean defect (MD) values were recorded.

For patients whose follow-up data were collected from community-based ophthalmologists, standardized documentation forms were provided to ensure consistent recording of key parameters, including IOP, best-corrected visual acuity (BCVA), OCT findings, visual field results, and relevant surgical or treatment history.

### Outcome Definitions

The primary endpoints were complete success and qualified success. Complete success was defined as achievement of target IOP without the use of any glaucoma medication. Qualified success was defined as achievement of target IOP with or without glaucoma medication. Two IOP target definitions were applied:

- **Definition 1:** IOP ≤ 18 mmHg
- **Definition 2:** IOP ≤ 21 mmHg with ≥ 20% reduction from baseline IOP

For both definitions, additional criteria for success included IOP ≥ 5 mmHg, absence of vision loss, and no requirement for further glaucoma surgery. Hypotony maculopathy was defined as IOP ≤ 5 mmHg accompanied by macular folds on OCT.

Secondary endpoints included changes in IOP from baseline, changes in medication use, long-term complications, and the need for revision surgeries.

### Statistical Analysis

Kaplan-Meier survival analysis was used to assess cumulative success probability, with the log-rank test employed to compare survival distributions between the two surgical groups. Cross-sectional comparisons between groups were performed using the Mann-Whitney U test. Longitudinal within-group comparisons were conducted using the Wilcoxon signed-rank test. Fisher's exact test was used to evaluate differences in dichotomous variables. A two-sided P-value ≤ 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

## Results

### Patient Characteristics and Follow-Up

Of the original 62 randomized patients, 54 completed the initial 2-year follow-up (TE n = 31, CP n = 23). For the present long-term follow-up, 49 patients were contacted, and 28 were included in the final analysis (TE n = 15, CP n = 13). The patient flow is depicted in Figure 1. Mean follow-up duration was 11.9 ± 1.1 years in the trabeculectomy group and 11.0 ± 2.0 years in the canaloplasty group.

Baseline characteristics of the 11-year follow-up cohort are presented in Table 1. Median preoperative IOP was 20.0 mmHg (interquartile range [IQR] 19.0--25.0) in the TE group and 22.0 mmHg (IQR 20.0--26.3) in the CP group (P = 0.06). Median number of preoperative topical glaucoma medications was 3.5 (IQR 3.0--4.0) in the TE group and 3.0 (IQR 1.8--4.0) in the CP group (P = 0.09). Median baseline BCVA was 0.10 logMAR (IQR 0.00--0.28) in the TE group and 0.22 logMAR (IQR 0.08--0.40) in the CP group (P = 0.13). Baseline visual field mean defect was 10.43 dB in the TE group and 4.57 dB in the CP group.

### Primary Outcomes: Surgical Success

Complete and qualified success rates at the 11-year follow-up are presented in Table 2. Using Definition 1, complete success was achieved in 53.3% (8/15) of TE patients and 15.4% (2/13) of CP patients (Fisher's exact test, P = 0.06). Using Definition 2, complete success was achieved in 46.7% (7/15) of TE patients and 15.4% (2/13) of CP patients (P = 0.22).

Qualified success rates were 73.3% (11/15) for TE and 69.2% (9/13) for CP under Definition 1 (P = 1.0), and 66.7% (10/15) for TE and 76.9% (10/13) for CP under Definition 2 (P = 0.69).

Kaplan-Meier survival analysis (Figure 2) demonstrated a statistically significant difference in cumulative complete success probability between groups for Definition 1 (log-rank P = 0.03), with trabeculectomy showing higher sustained success. For Definition 2, no significant difference was observed (log-rank P = 0.09).

### Intraocular Pressure

IOP outcomes at follow-up are presented in Table 3. Median IOP at the 11-year follow-up was 10.0 mmHg (IQR 6.0--12.0) in the TE group and 14.0 mmHg (IQR 11.5--17.75) in the CP group (P < 0.01). Mean IOP reduction from baseline was 11.6 ± 10.1 mmHg in the TE group and 9.4 ± 5.2 mmHg in the CP group.

### Medication Use

The mean number of glaucoma medications at follow-up was 1.0 ± 1.4 in the TE group and 1.9 ± 1.5 in the CP group (P = 0.17). The mean reduction in number of compounds from baseline was 2.13 ± 1.6 in the TE group and 0.31 ± 1.7 in the CP group. Within-group analysis showed that the medication reduction was statistically significant in the TE group (Wilcoxon signed-rank test, P < 0.01) but not in the CP group (P = 0.47). The proportion of patients requiring at least one glaucoma medication was 33% (5/15) in the TE group and 77% (10/13) in the CP group (Fisher's exact test, P = 0.03).

### Visual and Structural Outcomes

Mean BCVA at follow-up was 0.34 logMAR in the TE group and 0.43 logMAR in the CP group (P = 0.52). Visual acuity declined modestly in both groups from baseline: from 0.10 to 0.34 logMAR in TE and from 0.22 to 0.43 logMAR in CP.

Visual field mean defect at follow-up was 14.28 dB in the TE group and 7.29 dB in the CP group (P < 0.05). At baseline, mean defect values were 10.43 dB (TE) and 4.57 dB (CP), indicating that the between-group difference in visual field severity was present preoperatively and persisted at long-term follow-up.

Mean RNFL thickness (G-value) assessed by SD-OCT at follow-up was 62.4 ± 19.1 micrometers in the TE group and 66.1 ± 24.3 micrometers in the CP group.

### Complications

Hypotony maculopathy, defined as IOP ≤ 5 mmHg with macular folds on OCT, was the only long-term complication observed. It occurred in 2 of 15 TE patients (13.3%) and in none of the 13 CP patients. No bleb-related complications were observed in either group.

In the original 2-year TVC study, early postoperative complications in the TE group included transient hypotony (37.5%), hypotony-related choroidal detachment (12.5%), and elevated IOP (25.0%). In the CP group, early complications included elevated IOP exceeding 25 mmHg (30.0%), hyphema of 1 mm or greater (23.3%), and hypotony with IOP below 5 mmHg (20.0%).

### Revision Surgeries

Further glaucoma surgery was required in 20.0% (3/15) of TE patients and 23.1% (3/13) of CP patients. The mean time to revision surgery was 63 ± 33.5 months in the TE group and 96 ± 43.6 months in the CP group.

## Discussion

### Summary of Key Findings

The present study reports the 11-year outcomes of the TVC study, providing the longest follow-up of a randomized comparison between trabeculectomy and canaloplasty in open-angle glaucoma. The principal finding is that trabeculectomy maintained a clinically and statistically significant advantage in IOP reduction and complete success rates over canaloplasty after more than a decade. Median IOP at follow-up was 4 mmHg lower in the TE group (10.0 vs. 14.0 mmHg, P < 0.01), and complete success without medication was achieved in 53.3% of TE versus 15.4% of CP eyes under Definition 1. However, qualified success rates -- permitting adjunctive medication -- were comparable between groups (73.3% vs. 69.2% for Definition 1), and canaloplasty exhibited a more favorable safety profile, with no cases of hypotony maculopathy.

### Comparison with the Original TVC Study and Temporal Trends

The 11-year results demonstrate a predictable decline in complete success rates compared with the 2-year data reported by Matlach et al. [1]. At 2 years, complete success under Definition 1 was 74.2% for TE and 39.1% for CP; at 11 years, these rates had decreased to 53.3% and 15.4%, respectively. The decline was more pronounced in the CP group, suggesting that the mechanism of canaloplasty -- maintaining physiological outflow through Schlemm's canal -- may be subject to greater attenuation over time, possibly due to progressive fibrotic changes in the canal or surrounding tissues. In contrast, the filtration bleb created by trabeculectomy, though vulnerable to early failure, appears to sustain IOP control more durably in those eyes where it survives the initial postoperative period. Notably, the between-group IOP difference remained stable from 2 to 11 years (approximately 3--4 mmHg), indicating that the relative efficacy advantage of trabeculectomy was established early and persisted.

### Medication Burden and Clinical Implications

A distinguishing feature of the long-term outcomes was the significant difference in medication burden. Only 33% of TE patients required any glaucoma medication at 11 years compared with 77% of CP patients (P = 0.03). Furthermore, the mean reduction in the number of compounds from baseline was significant only in the TE group (2.13 ± 1.6, P < 0.01) and not in the CP group (0.31 ± 1.7, P = 0.47). This finding has important clinical implications: medication-free control reduces the long-term burden of chronic topical therapy, which is associated with ocular surface disease, reduced quality of life, and impaired adherence. For patients in whom complete medication independence is a primary treatment goal -- particularly younger patients facing decades of potential therapy -- trabeculectomy may offer a distinct advantage. Conversely, for patients who accept the use of one to two adjunctive medications and prioritize a lower complication risk, canaloplasty achieves comparable qualified success with a more favorable safety profile.

### Safety and Complication Profile

The safety data at 11 years reinforce the established risk-benefit difference between the two procedures. Hypotony maculopathy, a potentially vision-threatening complication, occurred exclusively in the trabeculectomy group (13.3%, 2/15 patients). This rate is consistent with published literature, in which hypotony maculopathy following trabeculectomy with MMC has been reported in 4--7% of cases. No cases of bleb-related infection or late bleb leak were observed, which is reassuring given that bleb-related complications have been reported in 1.6--23% of trabeculectomized eyes in the literature. The absence of any long-term complications in the canaloplasty group supports the favorable safety profile of this non-penetrating procedure. In the early postoperative period of the original TVC study, transient hyphema was a common canaloplasty-specific complication, occurring in 23.3% of cases, which is consistent with reported rates of 21--40% in the broader literature. However, this complication is typically self-limiting and did not appear to affect long-term outcomes.

### Revision Surgery

The rates of additional glaucoma surgery were similar between groups (TE 20.0% vs. CP 23.1%), though the timing differed: the mean time to revision was 63 ± 33.5 months in TE and 96 ± 43.6 months in CP. This suggests that failures necessitating reoperation tended to occur earlier after trabeculectomy, possibly related to early bleb fibrosis or hypotony-related interventions, whereas canaloplasty failures presented more gradually. Regardless of the timing, the finding that approximately one in five patients in each group required additional surgery underscores the importance of long-term monitoring after either procedure.

### Study Strengths

This study has several notable strengths. It represents the longest follow-up of a randomized comparison between trabeculectomy and canaloplasty reported to date, with a mean follow-up exceeding 11 years. The prospective, randomized design of the original TVC trial minimizes selection bias. Standardized examination protocols, including dual IOP measurements by Goldmann applanation tonometry, SD-OCT assessment with quality controls, and structured visual field testing, were employed to ensure measurement consistency. The use of standardized documentation forms for community-based practitioners further reduced data collection variability.

### Limitations

Several limitations should be acknowledged. The small sample size (15 TE, 13 CP) limits the statistical power of between-group comparisons and reduces the generalizability of the findings. Substantial attrition from the original cohort -- approximately 50% of the 2-year completers could not be followed -- introduces potential survival and selection bias, as patients lost to follow-up may have had systematically different outcomes. The challenges inherent to long-term follow-up, including patient relocation, declining health, and mortality, contributed to this attrition. The reliance on data collected by community-based ophthalmologists for some patients, despite the use of standardized forms, may have introduced measurement variability. No washout of glaucoma medications was performed before IOP assessment, which may have affected baseline and follow-up IOP measurements. Finally, the study lacked sufficient statistical power for some comparisons, as reflected by the discordance between the Fisher's exact test (P = 0.06) and the Kaplan-Meier log-rank test (P = 0.03) for complete success under Definition 1.

---

## Conclusions

After a mean follow-up exceeding 11 years, both trabeculectomy and canaloplasty demonstrated durable intraocular pressure reduction in patients with open-angle glaucoma. Trabeculectomy achieved significantly lower IOP (median 10.0 vs. 14.0 mmHg, P < 0.01) and higher complete success rates (53.3% vs. 15.4% by Kaplan-Meier analysis, P = 0.03), with a significantly greater reduction in medication burden. However, trabeculectomy was associated with a higher complication rate, with hypotony maculopathy occurring exclusively in this group (13.3%). Qualified success rates were comparable between procedures. For patients with moderately low target pressures who can tolerate some glaucoma medication use, canaloplasty remains a reliable and safer surgical alternative. These findings, representing the longest follow-up of a randomized trabeculectomy-canaloplasty comparison to date, support individualized surgical decision-making based on target IOP, acceptable medication burden, and risk tolerance.
