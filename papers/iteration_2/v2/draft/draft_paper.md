# Long-term Outcomes of Trabeculectomy versus Canaloplasty in Open-Angle Glaucoma: An 11-Year Follow-Up of the TVC Study Cohort

---

## Abstract

**Background:** The Trabeculectomy versus Canaloplasty (TVC) study was the first prospective randomized clinical trial directly comparing trabeculectomy (TE) and canaloplasty (CP) in patients with open-angle glaucoma (OAG). While the original 2-year results demonstrated superior intraocular pressure (IOP) reduction with trabeculectomy, long-term comparative data remain scarce.

**Objective:** To assess the long-term surgical outcomes of the TVC study cohort after 11 years of follow-up.

**Methods:** This prospective follow-up study included patients from the original 2015 TVC cohort (ClinicalTrials.gov: NCT01228799). Of 49 patients contacted from the original cohort (TE n=31, CP n=23), 28 were included (TE n=15, CP n=13). Primary endpoints were complete success (without glaucoma medication) and qualified success (with or without glaucoma medication), defined as IOP <=18 mmHg (Definition 1) or IOP <=21 mmHg with >=20% reduction from baseline (Definition 2). Secondary endpoints included IOP changes, medication use, complications, and revision surgeries. Kaplan-Meier analysis with log-rank testing, Mann-Whitney U test, Wilcoxon signed-rank test, and Fisher's exact test were used.

**Results:** Mean follow-up was 11.9 +/- 1.1 years (TE) and 11.0 +/- 2.0 years (CP). Complete success rates for TE versus CP were 53.3% versus 15.4% (Definition 1, P=0.06) and 46.7% versus 15.4% (Definition 2, P=0.22). Kaplan-Meier analysis showed a significant difference for Definition 1 (P=0.03). Qualified success rates were comparable: 73.3% versus 69.2% (Definition 1, P=1.0) and 66.7% versus 76.9% (Definition 2, P=0.69). Median IOP was 10.0 mmHg (IQR 6.0-12.0) for TE and 14.0 mmHg (IQR 11.5-17.75) for CP (P<0.01). Hypotony maculopathy occurred in 13.3% of TE patients and 0% of CP patients.

**Conclusions:** After 11 years, trabeculectomy demonstrated greater IOP reduction and higher complete success rates but was associated with a higher complication rate. For patients with moderately low target IOPs who can tolerate some glaucoma medication use, canaloplasty remains a reliable and safer alternative.

---

## Introduction

Glaucoma is a leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide, with open-angle glaucoma (OAG) accounting for the majority of cases in populations of European descent [1]. The primary modifiable risk factor is elevated intraocular pressure (IOP), and surgical intervention is indicated when medical and laser therapies fail to achieve adequate IOP control [2]. Trabeculectomy (TE), first described by Cairns in 1968 [2], remains the most frequently performed glaucoma surgery and is widely considered the gold standard for IOP reduction. However, trabeculectomy is a penetrating procedure associated with significant complications including bleb-related infections, hypotony, and hypotony maculopathy [8, 14].

The recognition of these complications has driven the development of less invasive, non-penetrating surgical approaches that avoid the creation of a filtering bleb [6, 7, 12]. Canaloplasty (CP), an ab externo procedure involving circumferential viscodilation and tensioning of Schlemm's canal, emerged as a promising alternative that enhances physiological aqueous outflow [3, 8, 13]. Several multicenter studies have demonstrated that canaloplasty achieves sustained IOP reduction with a favorable safety profile, though typically with less absolute IOP lowering than trabeculectomy [4, 5].

Despite the availability of short- to medium-term comparative data, long-term evidence directly comparing these two surgical approaches from randomized trials has been lacking. The original TVC (Trabeculectomy versus Canaloplasty) study, published by Matlach et al. in 2015, was the first head-to-head prospective randomized clinical trial comparing trabeculectomy and canaloplasty in patients with open-angle glaucoma [1]. Over 24 months of follow-up, the study demonstrated that trabeculectomy achieved greater IOP reduction and higher complete success rates (74.2% vs. 39.1% for Definition 1, P=0.01) but at the cost of a higher complication rate, including transient hypotony in 37.5% of trabeculectomy patients [1].

The present study aimed to assess the long-term outcomes of the TVC study cohort after 11 years, providing the longest follow-up of a randomized comparison between trabeculectomy and canaloplasty to date. Such long-term data are critical for informing surgical decision-making and guiding evidence-based clinical practice in glaucoma management.

## Methods

### Study Design and Participants

This was a prospective follow-up study of the original TVC study cohort. The original TVC study was a prospective, single-center, randomized clinical trial conducted at the Department of Ophthalmology, University Hospital Wuerzburg, Germany (ClinicalTrials.gov Identifier: NCT01228799). In the original trial, 62 patients with uncontrolled open-angle glaucoma were randomized to undergo either trabeculectomy (n=32) or canaloplasty (n=30) and were followed prospectively for 2 years [1].

For the present long-term follow-up, the starting cohort consisted of patients who had completed the original 2-year follow-up period (TE n=31, CP n=23). Patients were excluded from the current analysis if they had confirmed death (via case files or confirmation by relatives), refused to participate, or had an unknown address. Of 49 patients contacted, 28 were included in the final analysis (TE n=15, CP n=13). Follow-up data were obtained either through visits to the department (TE n=10, CP n=11) or from local ophthalmologists using standardized documentation forms (TE n=3, CP n=4). The patient flow is detailed in Figure 1.

The Department of Statistics at the University of Wuerzburg calculated the sample size, determining that 50% of the original TVC 2015 cohort was required to ensure 80% power at a 0.05 significance level.

### Ethics Statement

The original TVC study was registered at ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT01228799) and was conducted at the University Hospital Wuerzburg. Ethics approval and informed consent were obtained as required for clinical research involving human participants.

### Surgical Techniques

Trabeculectomy was performed with intraoperative application of mitomycin C (MMC). Canaloplasty was performed as an ab externo procedure with a tensioning suture placed in Schlemm's canal. In the original 2-year study, antiglaucomatous drugs were added in cases of elevated IOP after failed laser goniopuncture in canaloplasty patients and after scleral flap revision or bleb needling in trabeculectomy patients.

### Examination Protocol

Intraocular pressure was measured on two separate occasions using Goldmann applanation tonometry with a one-hour interval between measurements. One measurement was obtained in miosis and the other after instillation of tropicamide eye drops. Consistent with the original TVC study, no washout of glaucoma medications was performed at baseline.

Spectral-domain optical coherence tomography (SD-OCT) was performed using the Heidelberg Spectralis OCT (Heidelberg Engineering, Heidelberg, Germany). The G-value of the retinal nerve fiber layer (RNFL) scan was used as a descriptive structural outcome. Only scans with a quality (Q) value exceeding 20 were included.

Visual field testing was conducted using a static perimeter with the 30-2 program (Octopus 900, Haag-Streit, Switzerland).

For patients whose data were collected from external ophthalmologists, standardized forms were provided to ensure consistent documentation of key parameters, including IOP, visual acuity, OCT findings, visual field results, and relevant surgical or treatment history.

### Outcome Definitions

Primary endpoints were complete success (IOP within target range without glaucoma medication) and qualified success (IOP within target range with or without glaucoma medication). Two IOP target definitions were used: Definition 1, IOP <=18 mmHg; and Definition 2, IOP <=21 mmHg with >=20% reduction from baseline. Additional criteria for success required IOP >=5 mmHg, no vision loss, and no further glaucoma surgery.

Secondary endpoints included changes in IOP, medication use, complications, and revision surgeries.

### Statistical Analysis

Kaplan-Meier analysis was used to assess cumulative success probability. The log-rank test was employed to compare survival distributions between groups. The Mann-Whitney U test was used for cross-sectional comparisons, and the Wilcoxon signed-rank test was used for longitudinal (within-group) comparisons. Fisher's exact test was used for differences in dichotomous variables. A P-value <=0.05 was considered statistically significant.

## Results

### Patient Cohort and Baseline Characteristics

The final cohort consisted of 15 trabeculectomy patients and 13 canaloplasty patients (Table 1). Mean follow-up was 11.9 +/- 1.1 years for trabeculectomy and 11.0 +/- 2.0 years for canaloplasty. All patients were Caucasian, and glaucoma subtypes included primary open-angle glaucoma (POAG), pigmentary glaucoma (PG), and pseudoexfoliation glaucoma (PEXG).

Baseline parameters were comparable between groups. Median baseline IOP was 20.0 mmHg (IQR 19.0-25.0) in the trabeculectomy group and 22.0 mmHg (IQR 20.0-26.3) in the canaloplasty group (P=0.06). Median baseline number of topical glaucoma medications was 3.5 (IQR 3.0-4.0) in the trabeculectomy group and 3.0 (IQR 1.8-4.0) in the canaloplasty group (P=0.09). Baseline best-corrected visual acuity (BCVA) was 0.10 logMAR (IQR 0.00-0.28) in the trabeculectomy group and 0.22 logMAR (IQR 0.08-0.40) in the canaloplasty group (P=0.13). Baseline visual field (VF) mean defect was 10.43 dB in the trabeculectomy group and 4.57 dB in the canaloplasty group. At the time of surgery, 6 patients (18.8%) in the trabeculectomy group and 5 patients (16.7%) in the canaloplasty group were pseudophakic (based on original cohort sizes of TE n=32 and CP n=30).

### Primary Outcomes: Success Rates

Complete success (without medication) according to Definition 1 (IOP <=18 mmHg) was achieved in 53.3% (8/15) of trabeculectomy patients and 15.4% (2/13) of canaloplasty patients (P=0.06). For Definition 2 (IOP <=21 mmHg with >=20% reduction from baseline), complete success was 46.7% (7/15) for trabeculectomy and 15.4% (2/13) for canaloplasty (P=0.22).

Qualified success (with or without medication) according to Definition 1 was 73.3% (11/15) for trabeculectomy and 69.2% (9/13) for canaloplasty (P=1.0). For Definition 2, qualified success was 66.7% (10/15) for trabeculectomy and 76.9% (10/13) for canaloplasty (P=0.69). Overall success rates were 73.3% for trabeculectomy and 76.9% for canaloplasty.

### Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis

Kaplan-Meier survival analysis for complete success showed a statistically significant difference between groups for Definition 1 (P=0.03, log-rank test), with trabeculectomy maintaining a higher cumulative success probability over the 11-year follow-up period (Figure 2, Panel A). For Definition 2, the difference between groups did not reach statistical significance (P=0.09; Figure 2, Panel B).

### IOP Outcomes

Median IOP at follow-up was 10.0 mmHg (IQR 6.0-12.0) in the trabeculectomy group and 14.0 mmHg (IQR 11.5-17.75) in the canaloplasty group (P<0.01, Mann-Whitney U test; Figure 3). Mean IOP reduction from baseline was 11.6 +/- 10.1 mmHg for trabeculectomy and 9.4 +/- 5.2 mmHg for canaloplasty.

### Medication Use

The mean number of topical glaucoma medication compounds at follow-up was 1.0 +/- 1.4 in the trabeculectomy group and 1.9 +/- 1.5 in the canaloplasty group (P=0.17). The absolute reduction in the number of compounds from baseline was 2.13 +/- 1.6 for trabeculectomy and 0.31 +/- 1.7 for canaloplasty. Within-group analysis showed a statistically significant reduction in medication use for trabeculectomy (P<0.01, Wilcoxon signed-rank test) but not for canaloplasty (P=0.47). The proportion of patients requiring at least one glaucoma medication was 33% (5/15) in the trabeculectomy group and 77% (10/13) in the canaloplasty group (P=0.03, Fisher's exact test).

### Visual Outcomes

Mean BCVA at follow-up was 0.34 logMAR in the trabeculectomy group and 0.43 logMAR in the canaloplasty group. BCVA worsened from baseline in both groups (trabeculectomy: 0.10 to 0.34 logMAR; canaloplasty: 0.22 to 0.43 logMAR), with no significant difference between groups (P=0.52). Visual field mean defect at follow-up was 14.28 dB in the trabeculectomy group and 7.29 dB in the canaloplasty group (P<0.05), compared with baseline values of 10.43 dB and 4.57 dB, respectively.

### Structural Outcomes

Mean retinal nerve fiber layer (RNFL) thickness (G-value) at follow-up was 62.4 +/- 19.1 um in the trabeculectomy group and 66.1 +/- 24.3 um in the canaloplasty group.

### Complications

Hypotony maculopathy, defined as IOP <=5 mmHg with macular folds on OCT, was observed in 13.3% (2/15) of trabeculectomy patients and 0% (0/13) of canaloplasty patients. No bleb-related complications were observed in either group. In the original 2-year TVC study, early postoperative complications in the trabeculectomy group included transient hypotony (37.5%), hypotony-related choroidal detachment (12.5%), and elevated IOP (25.0%). In the canaloplasty group, early postoperative complications included elevated IOP >25 mmHg (30%), hyphema >=1 mm (23.3%), and hypotony with IOP <5 mmHg (20%).

### Revision Surgeries

Subsequent glaucoma surgery was performed in 20.0% (3/15) of trabeculectomy patients and 23.1% (3/13) of canaloplasty patients. The mean time to revision surgery was 63 +/- 33.5 months in the trabeculectomy group and 96 +/- 43.6 months in the canaloplasty group.

## Discussion

### Summary of Key Findings

After a mean follow-up exceeding 11 years, this study demonstrated that both trabeculectomy and canaloplasty provide durable IOP reduction in patients with open-angle glaucoma. Trabeculectomy achieved greater absolute IOP reduction (median 10.0 vs. 14.0 mmHg, P<0.01) and a substantially higher rate of medication-free success (53.3% vs. 15.4% for Definition 1). However, this superior efficacy was accompanied by an increased risk of hypotony maculopathy, which occurred exclusively in the trabeculectomy group (13.3%). Qualified success rates were comparable between groups (73.3% vs. 69.2% for Definition 1), indicating that when adjunctive medications are permitted, canaloplasty achieves similar overall IOP control to trabeculectomy.

### Comparison with the Original TVC Study

The present findings are consistent with and extend the original 2-year TVC results reported by Matlach et al. [1]. In the original study, complete success rates for trabeculectomy versus canaloplasty were 74.2% versus 39.1% (Definition 1, P=0.01) at 2 years. At 11 years, complete success declined to 53.3% versus 15.4%, reflecting the expected attrition of surgical success over time while maintaining the directional superiority of trabeculectomy. The sustained separation in IOP between groups (mean IOP reduction 11.6 vs. 9.4 mmHg at 11 years, compared with 10.8 vs. 9.3 mmHg at 2 years) suggests stable, long-term efficacy differences between the two procedures. Notably, the mean IOP reduction for trabeculectomy appeared slightly greater at 11 years than at 2 years, which may reflect survivor bias, as patients with failed surgery were more likely to have undergone additional interventions or dropped out.

### Comparison with Other Surgical Series

The long-term efficacy observed in the present study is broadly consistent with published data from other surgical series. Landers et al. reported 57% complete success and 88% qualified success after 20 years of trabeculectomy follow-up in 234 patients [8], which aligns with the qualified success rate of 73.3% observed in the present study at 11 years. For canaloplasty, Grieshaber et al. reported sustained IOP reduction with mean IOP values of 15.4 mmHg at 5 years and 15.5 mmHg at 10 years in a large series of over 700 viscocanalostomy cases [10], comparable to the median IOP of 14.0 mmHg observed in the canaloplasty group in the present study. Lewis et al. reported mean IOP reduction from 23.8 to 15.2 mmHg at 3 years in 157 canaloplasty patients [4], and Bull et al. reported IOP reduction from 23.0 to 15.1 mmHg at 3 years in a European multicenter study [5], both consistent with the moderate IOP-lowering effect observed here at 11 years. Bruggemann et al. found comparable IOP reduction between canaloplasty (50.3%) and trabeculectomy (53.4%, P=0.87) at 12 months in a unique bilateral intraindividual comparison [6], though canaloplasty required substantially fewer postoperative interventions (2 vs. 112).

### Medication Use and Complete Success

A particularly notable finding was the significant difference in medication burden between groups. Only 33% of trabeculectomy patients required at least one glaucoma medication at 11 years, compared with 77% of canaloplasty patients (P=0.03). This is clinically meaningful because medication-free outcomes reduce the burden of chronic therapy, improve adherence, reduce ocular surface disease associated with preservative-containing drops, and may improve patient quality of life. Klink et al. previously demonstrated that canaloplasty patients reported higher quality of life (57% highly satisfied vs. 41% for trabeculectomy, P=0.034) and lower stress related to postoperative care [7], though this advantage may be partially offset by the greater long-term reliance on topical medications. The Kaplan-Meier survival analysis reinforced this finding, with a statistically significant difference for complete success under Definition 1 (P=0.03, log-rank test), though the difference for Definition 2 did not reach significance (P=0.09).

### Revision Surgeries and Timing

The revision surgery rates were comparable between groups (trabeculectomy 20.0% vs. canaloplasty 23.1%), though the timing of revisions differed substantially. Revisions occurred earlier in the trabeculectomy group (mean 63 +/- 33.5 months) compared with the canaloplasty group (mean 96 +/- 43.6 months). This temporal difference may reflect different mechanisms of failure: trabeculectomy failure typically involves bleb fibrosis and scarring that tends to occur within the first few years, whereas canaloplasty failure may be related to more gradual processes such as progressive trabecular meshwork dysfunction or suture-related changes. In the Tube Versus Trabeculectomy (TVT) study, a 29% reoperation rate was reported for trabeculectomy over 5 years [9], which is broadly comparable to the rate observed in the present study. Bruggemann et al. similarly reported that canaloplasty required far fewer postoperative interventions than trabeculectomy [6].

### Safety Profile and Complications

The complication profile observed in the present study is consistent with the known safety differences between these procedures. Hypotony maculopathy was the only long-term complication and occurred exclusively in the trabeculectomy group (13.3%, 2/15 patients). This rate falls within the reported range of 1.3% to 18% for hypotony maculopathy after trabeculectomy in the published literature [14]. Importantly, no bleb-related complications were observed in the trabeculectomy group at 11 years, which is reassuring given that bleb-related complications have been reported in 1.6% to 23% of trabeculectomy patients in other series [14]. The absence of long-term complications in the canaloplasty group confirms the favorable safety profile of this non-penetrating procedure, consistent with the low complication rates reported in the canaloplasty literature [3, 4, 5].

### Baseline Differences and Potential Confounders

An important consideration in interpreting the visual field results is the baseline difference in VF mean defect between groups (trabeculectomy 10.43 dB vs. canaloplasty 4.57 dB). This difference, present already at baseline, was maintained at follow-up (14.28 vs. 7.29 dB, P<0.05). The more advanced glaucomatous damage at baseline in the trabeculectomy group may have influenced the pattern of disease progression and should be considered a potential confounder when comparing functional outcomes between groups. Despite randomization, this imbalance could reflect small-sample variability or differential dropout patterns in the long-term cohort.

### Strengths

This study has several notable strengths. First, it represents the longest follow-up of a randomized comparison between trabeculectomy and canaloplasty, providing unique data on the durability of both procedures beyond a decade. Second, the study maintained prospective data collection using standardized examination protocols, including Goldmann applanation tonometry, Heidelberg Spectralis OCT, and Octopus 900 visual field testing, enhancing the reliability and comparability of the outcome data. Third, the use of standardized forms for community-based practitioners helped maintain data quality even when patients could not attend the hospital for follow-up. Fourth, the original randomized design of the TVC study reduces selection bias in the initial treatment allocation. Fifth, the analysis achieved the pre-specified sample size requirement of 50% of the original cohort, as determined by the Department of Statistics at the University of Wuerzburg to ensure 80% power at a 0.05 significance level.

## Limitations

Several limitations should be considered. First, the small sample size (15 trabeculectomy, 13 canaloplasty patients) limits generalizability and statistical power. Several comparisons that showed numerical differences between groups (e.g., complete success under Definition 1, P=0.06) may have reached statistical significance with a larger sample. Second, substantial attrition occurred from the original cohort (62 to 28 patients, approximately 55% loss to follow-up), which is inherent to long-term surgical follow-up studies but may introduce survivor bias. Third, the challenges of collecting long-term outcome data -- including patient relocation, inability to visit due to declining health, and mortality -- are unavoidable but may affect the representativeness of the remaining cohort. Fourth, reliance on community-based practitioner data for some patients (TE n=3, CP n=4) may have introduced measurement variability despite the use of standardized documentation forms. Fifth, no washout of glaucoma medications was performed prior to IOP measurement, which may have influenced the IOP values, particularly in the canaloplasty group where 77% of patients were using at least one medication. Sixth, the baseline difference in visual field mean defect between groups (TE 10.43 dB vs. CP 4.57 dB) represents a potential confounder, as it suggests that the trabeculectomy group had more advanced glaucomatous damage at baseline, which may have influenced disease progression and complication rates.

---

## Conclusions

At an average follow-up exceeding 11 years, both trabeculectomy and canaloplasty demonstrated durable intraocular pressure reduction in patients with open-angle glaucoma. Trabeculectomy demonstrated greater IOP reduction (median 10.0 vs. 14.0 mmHg, P<0.01) and a higher rate of medication-free complete success (53.3% vs. 15.4% for IOP <=18 mmHg, P=0.03 on Kaplan-Meier analysis), but was associated with a higher complication rate, including hypotony maculopathy in 13.3% of patients. Qualified success rates were comparable between groups. For patients with moderately low target IOPs who can tolerate some glaucoma medication use, canaloplasty remains a reliable and safer alternative. To the best of the authors' knowledge, this study reports the longest follow-up of a randomized comparison between trabeculectomy and canaloplasty, underscoring the lasting relevance of both procedures in glaucoma surgery and the critical role of long-term data in shaping evidence-based clinical decisions.
