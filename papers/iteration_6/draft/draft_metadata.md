# Draft Metadata: Writing Decisions and Assumptions

## Working Title
"Systemic and Ocular Comorbidities Associated With Keratoconus: A Retrospective Matched Case-Control Study Using a National Health Research Database"

**Rationale:** The title was constructed to be descriptive of the study content—emphasizing the case-control design, national database setting, and the broad scope of comorbidities examined. No attempt was made to identify or replicate an original title.

---

## Structural Decisions

### Abstract
- Structured format with Purpose, Methods, Results, and Conclusions subheadings.
- Word count: approximately 230 words, within the 200–250 target.
- All four statistically significant multivariable associations plus the inverse smoking association were reported with exact ORs, CIs, and P values.

### Introduction
- Four paragraphs following the background-gap-objective arc.
- Paragraph 1: General KC epidemiology and burden.
- Paragraph 2: Known multifactorial pathogenesis including environmental, hormonal, and systemic factors.
- Paragraph 3: Identified gap—limited comprehensive, multivariable analyses using nationally representative data.
- Paragraph 4: Study aim stated using objective/aim language appropriate for an observational study (no hypothesis language).
- All clinical background statements hedged and marked with [REF] placeholders.

### Methods
- Preserved all subsections from the extracted data: Study Design and Data Source, Case and Control Selection, Clinical Variables, Statistical Analysis.
- Reported matching variables (age ±1 year, ethnicity, sex) and the 1:3 matching ratio.
- Described the female-only subanalysis as a separate component.
- Noted the composite definition of ocular surface disease.
- Described the hybrid smoking definition (survey + ICD codes).

### Results
- Strictly descriptive language used throughout; no interpretive phrases such as "suggesting that" or "indicating that."
- Demographics section confirmed matching adequacy by reporting P values for matched variables (all P = 1.0).
- Race was reported as an unmatched covariate with its P value (>0.99).
- Multivariable results reported with prevalence percentages, ORs, 95% CIs, and exact P values.
- Non-significant results reported individually with exact P values rather than grouping them.
- Percentages in the multivariable results section were taken directly from the extracted data (e.g., "57.87%," "44.76%"), assumed to represent the prevalence of each condition among KC cases.

### Discussion
- Six thematic paragraphs: (1) Ocular surface disease, (2) Obesity and metabolic factors, (3) Hormonal influences, (4) Tetracycline, (5) Smoking, (6) Vitamin C and other non-significant factors.
- Separate Strengths and Limitations subsections.
- Standalone Conclusions subsection.
- Hedging language used throughout (e.g., "may suggest," "appears to," "could contribute").
- No first-person language; "the present study" used consistently.

## Key Assumptions

1. **Percentages in multivariable results:** The extracted data reported percentages alongside ORs (e.g., "ocular surface disease (57.87%; OR = 6.04...)"). These were assumed to represent the prevalence of each condition among the KC cohort, not among controls or the total sample. They were reported as such in the Results section.

2. **Univariable results unavailability:** The extracted data stated that univariable analyses were performed but "were not extractable from the available source." This was acknowledged as a limitation. The guideline to report both univariable and multivariable estimates could not be fully satisfied due to data unavailability.

3. **Ungrouped ocular surface disease analysis:** Similarly noted as performed but unavailable for inclusion. This was flagged in the Limitations section.

4. **Female-only subanalysis context:** Estrogen-containing medication and pregnancy were analyzed in a female-only subgroup (n = 316 KC cases, n = 948 controls). This was clearly stated in the Methods section. In the Results, estrogen-containing medication results were presented in the main multivariable table, consistent with the extracted data presentation.

5. **Database identity:** The data source was described generically as "a large national health research database" consistent with the extracted data. No attempt was made to identify the specific database.

6. **Race as an unmatched variable:** Although matching was performed on age, ethnicity, and sex (all P = 1.0), race was not a matching variable. The slight differences in racial distribution between groups (e.g., Black or African American: 23% KC vs. 18% controls) were noted in the Limitations section as potential residual confounding, even though the overall race comparison P value was >0.99.

7. **Statistical significance threshold:** Assumed to be P < 0.05, as is conventional, based on the pattern of which variables were described as "statistically significant" in the extracted data.

8. **ICD code specifics:** The extracted data mentioned "standardized medical terminology and ICD codes" without specifying ICD-9 vs. ICD-10. The Methods section used the general term "ICD codes" accordingly.

9. **Ethics and IRB:** The extracted data stated that data were "de-identified and made accessible to registered researchers" but did not mention IRB approval. The Methods section noted compliance with "ethical standards of the database platform and applicable regulations" without fabricating IRB details.

## Tense Usage

- **Methods and Results:** Past tense throughout (e.g., "was conducted," "were matched," "was associated").
- **Introduction and Discussion:** Present tense for established facts and general knowledge (e.g., "Keratoconus is a progressive corneal ectasia"); past tense when referencing the present study's findings in the Discussion.
