# Ocular Safety of 222-nm Far-Ultraviolet C Irradiation at Doses Up to 75 mJ/cm2: A Prospective Interventional Study With Six-Month Follow-Up

[Author et al.]

[Institution]

---

## Abstract

**Purpose:** To evaluate the ocular safety of 222-nm far-ultraviolet C (far-UVC) irradiation from a filtered krypton chloride (KrCl) excimer lamp at doses of 22, 50, and 75 mJ/cm2 in healthy human subjects over a six-month follow-up period.

**Methods:** A prospective, interventional study was conducted at a university hospital ophthalmology department. Five subjects (aged 29-47 years; mean +/- SD: 40.0 +/- 7.3 years; 4 males, 1 female) received 222-nm far-UVC irradiation to the right eye at doses of 22 mJ/cm2 (n=1), 50 mJ/cm2 (n=2), or 75 mJ/cm2 (n=2), with the left eye serving as an unirradiated control via custom-made glasses with a UV-cut filter. A stepwise safety design was employed, with higher doses administered only after confirming no significant ocular damage at preceding dose levels. Outcome measures included best-corrected visual acuity (BCVA), spherical equivalent refractive error (SERE), corneal erosion scores, conjunctival hyperemia score, corneal endothelial cell density (CECD), and the presence of pterygium, cataract, or facial skin changes. Subjects were assessed at baseline, 1 day, 1 month, 3 months, and 6 months post-irradiation.

**Results:** No clinically significant changes in BCVA, SERE, conjunctival hyperemia, or CECD were observed in either eye of any subject through 6 months. Transient corneal erosion (area and density scores of 1) was observed in two subjects at the 75 mJ/cm2 dose at 1 day, resolving by 1 month. Mild self-limited ocular symptoms (epiphora sensation, discomfort, mild pain) were reported in the irradiated eye only, beginning 2-3 hours after irradiation onset and resolving within 4.5-11 hours. No pterygium, cataract, or facial skin changes were detected.

**Conclusions:** Far-UVC irradiation at 222 nm at doses up to 75 mJ/cm2 did not produce lasting ocular damage in this pilot study. Transient corneal effects at the highest dose warrant further investigation in larger cohorts to establish safe exposure thresholds.

## Introduction

The ongoing need for effective germicidal technologies in healthcare, public, and occupational settings has driven interest in ultraviolet C (UVC) irradiation as a tool for airborne and surface pathogen inactivation [REF]. Conventional germicidal UVC, typically emitted at 254 nm by low-pressure mercury lamps, is highly effective against bacteria and viruses but poses well-documented risks to the skin and eyes, including photokeratitis and potential long-term carcinogenic effects, which limits its use in occupied spaces [REF]. These safety concerns have historically restricted UVC deployment to unoccupied room disinfection or upper-room configurations designed to minimize direct human exposure.

Far-UVC radiation, defined as wavelengths in the 200-230 nm range, has emerged as a potentially safer alternative. The physical basis for this safety claim is that far-UVC photons are strongly absorbed by the outermost dead-cell layers of the stratum corneum and the tear film overlying the corneal epithelium, limiting penetration to living cells [REF]. Among far-UVC sources, filtered KrCl excimer lamps emitting at 222 nm have received particular attention, as they combine germicidal efficacy against a broad spectrum of pathogens with the theoretical advantage of reduced tissue penetration [REF]. Several in vitro and animal studies have supported the safety of 222-nm irradiation at moderate doses, reporting no significant corneal damage in murine models [REF]. However, the translational gap between animal eyes and human eyes is substantial; differences in corneal thickness, tear film composition, and blink dynamics necessitate direct human safety data.

Human evidence on ocular safety of far-UVC exposure remains limited. Existing threshold limit values (TLVs) and exposure guidelines for UVC radiation were established primarily based on 254-nm data and may not directly apply to the 222-nm wavelength [REF]. A small number of studies have investigated skin safety of 222-nm exposure in humans, but prospective studies evaluating comprehensive ocular endpoints -- including visual acuity, corneal integrity, endothelial cell density, and long-term sequelae such as pterygium and cataract -- are scarce. This evidence gap hinders the safe deployment of far-UVC systems in occupied environments where direct ocular exposure may occur.

The present study aimed to evaluate the ocular safety of 222-nm far-UVC irradiation from a filtered KrCl excimer lamp at escalating doses of 22, 50, and 75 mJ/cm2 in healthy human subjects, using a prospective, interventional design with the contralateral eye as an internal control and follow-up extending to six months.

## Methods

### Study Design and Ethics

This was a prospective, interventional study conducted at the ophthalmology department of a university hospital ([Institution]). Subjects were enrolled to receive escalating doses of 222-nm far-UVC irradiation following a stepwise safety design. The study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and approved by the institutional review board ([Institution]). Written informed consent was obtained from all participants prior to enrollment.

### Participants

Five subjects aged 29-47 years (mean +/- SD: 40.0 +/- 7.3 years; 4 males, 1 female) were recruited. Inclusion criteria were: age 20-49 years; ability to avoid excessive UV exposure within 48 hours before irradiation and follow-up visits; decimal BCVA of 0.9 or better in both eyes; uncorrected binocular visual acuity (VA) of 0.3 or better; no ocular pathologies affecting VA; and no use of topical eye or facial skin medications. Exclusion criteria were: contact lens wear; daily occupational UV exposure; corneal erosion density score >=2 or conjunctival hyperemia score >=2 prior to irradiation; dry eye disease; atopic dermatitis; sunlight sensitivity; and participation in other clinical trials.

### Irradiation Apparatus and Protocol

The irradiation source was a mercury-free KrCl excimer lamp unit emitting at 222 nm, equipped with a cut-off filter that attenuated wavelengths longer than 230 nm and completely blocked wavelengths longer than 240 nm (Figure 2). Spectral impurity, defined as the ratio of 235-320 nm radiation to 200-230 nm radiation, was 0.55%. The lamp was positioned 200 cm from the subject's eyes at 40 cm above floor level, directed at eye level (Figure 1). Irradiance at the subject's eye position was 3.1-3.4 microW/cm2.

Each subject's left eye served as its own control through the use of custom-made glasses featuring no filter on the right side and a UV-cut filter on the left side, with the left-side filter transmitting approximately 0.3% of incident 222-nm radiation (Figure 3). Subjects were assigned to one of three dose groups: 22 mJ/cm2 (n=1, 2 hours irradiation), 50 mJ/cm2 (n=2, 4 hours irradiation), or 75 mJ/cm2 (n=2, 6 hours irradiation). For the 50 and 75 mJ/cm2 groups, a 10-minute break was taken every 2 hours. During irradiation, subjects watched a movie on a TV monitor positioned 196 cm from the seating position, and were monitored via two cameras (one in front, one behind) to ensure continuous forward-facing gaze (Figure 4).

### Stepwise Safety Design

A dose-escalation protocol was employed: irradiation at 50 mJ/cm2 was only administered after confirming no significant ocular damage at 1 month following the 22 mJ/cm2 exposure. Similarly, irradiation at 75 mJ/cm2 was only administered after confirming no significant ocular damage at 3 months post-22 mJ/cm2 or at 1 month post-50 mJ/cm2 exposure.

### Outcome Measures

The following parameters were assessed: BCVA (decimal notation), SERE (diopters, D), corneal erosion area score (0-3 scale), corneal erosion density score (0-3 scale), conjunctival hyperemia score (0-3 scale, per standard Japanese guidelines for allergic conjunctival disease), and CECD (cells/mm2). Corneal erosion was assessed with sodium-fluorescein staining under blue-light slit lamp examination (Figure 5). The presence or absence of pterygium, cataract, and facial skin changes (erythema, pigmentation) was recorded. Subjective symptoms within 24 hours of irradiation were also documented through interviews at the Day 1 visit.

### Follow-Up Schedule

Assessments were performed at baseline (within 1 week prior to irradiation), 1 day (24 hours after the start of irradiation), 1 month, 3 months, and 6 months post-irradiation. SERE and CECD measurements were omitted at the 1-day examination.

### Statistical Analysis

Given the small sample size (n=5) and exploratory nature of this study, outcomes were reported descriptively for each subject at each time point. Individual-level data for both the irradiated (right) and control (left) eyes were tabulated and compared within subjects across the follow-up period.

### Objective Ocular Safety Outcomes

Complete results for all five subjects across all time points are presented in Table 2. During the follow-up period from 1 day to 6 months, BCVA, SERE, conjunctival hyperemia score, and CECD did not show clinically meaningful changes in either eye of any subject. Pterygium, cataract, and facial skin changes (erythema, pigmentation) were not detected in any subject at any time point.

BCVA remained stable throughout follow-up. All subjects maintained decimal BCVA of 1.2 or better in both eyes at all measured time points (1 month, 3 months, 6 months). SERE values fluctuated within normal physiological ranges without systematic trends. CECD values remained within normal ranges for all subjects in both eyes across all measured time points.

Conjunctival hyperemia scores remained at 0 in both eyes for all five subjects at all time points from baseline through 6 months.

**Corneal Erosion Findings**

At the 22 mJ/cm2 dose, Subject 1 showed corneal erosion area and density scores of 1/1 (right/left) at baseline, which remained at 1/1 at Day 1 and 1 month in the right eye before decreasing to 0/0 at 3 and 6 months in both eyes.

At the 50 mJ/cm2 dose, Subject 2 showed corneal erosion area and density scores of 0 in both eyes at all time points. Subject 3 showed erosion area and density scores of 1/1 (right/left) at baseline, remaining at 1/1 at Day 1 and 1 month, decreasing to 0/0 at 3 months, and returning to 1/1 at 6 months in both eyes.

At the 75 mJ/cm2 dose, Subject 4 showed corneal erosion area and density scores of 0/0 at baseline, increasing to 1/0 (right/left) at Day 1, then returning to 0/0 at 1 month and remaining at 0 through 6 months. Subject 5 showed corneal erosion area and density scores of 0/1 (right/left) at baseline, with both eyes scoring 1/1 at Day 1; scores returned to 0/0 at 3 months in both eyes.

Thus, transient corneal erosion in the irradiated eye only, not present at baseline, was observed in two subjects receiving 75 mJ/cm2 (Subjects 4 and 5) at Day 1. In both cases, the scores were mild (area score 1, density score 1) and resolved by 1 month (Figure 5).

### Subject-Reported Symptoms

All five subjects reported ocular symptoms in the irradiated (right) eye within 24 hours of irradiation onset (Table 3). No symptoms were reported for the control (left) eye in any subject. Symptom onset ranged from 2 to 3 hours after the start of irradiation, and symptom duration ranged from 4.5 to 11 hours from the start of irradiation.

At the lowest dose (22 mJ/cm2), Subject 1 reported epiphora sensation beginning 2.5 hours after irradiation onset and lasting until 4.5 hours. At the 50 mJ/cm2 dose, Subject 2 reported dry eye sensation, mild photophobia, and epiphora sensation (onset 2 hours, duration to 7 hours); Subject 3 reported eye discomfort, mild photophobia, and epiphora sensation (onset 3 hours, duration to 6 hours). At the 75 mJ/cm2 dose, Subject 4 reported mild pain, epiphora, and conjunctival hyperemia (onset 2 hours, duration to 11 hours); Subject 5 reported eye discomfort, mild pain, and epiphora (onset 2 hours, duration to 10 hours).

A dose-dependent pattern was observed: higher doses were associated with a greater number and severity of symptoms and longer symptom duration.

## Discussion

### Summary of Key Findings

The present study evaluated the ocular safety of 222-nm far-UVC irradiation at doses of 22, 50, and 75 mJ/cm2 in five healthy human subjects over a six-month follow-up period. The principal finding was that no lasting ocular damage was detected at any dose level. BCVA, SERE, conjunctival hyperemia scores, and CECD remained stable and comparable between the irradiated and control eyes throughout the follow-up period. Pterygium, cataract, and facial skin changes were absent in all subjects. However, transient corneal erosion (area and density scores of 1) was observed in two subjects at the highest dose (75 mJ/cm2) at 1 day post-irradiation, resolving by 1 month. All subjects experienced self-limited ocular symptoms in the irradiated eye, with symptom severity and duration appearing to increase with dose.

### Comparison With Prior Literature

These findings are consistent with the growing body of evidence from animal and in vitro studies supporting the relative safety of filtered 222-nm far-UVC radiation compared with conventional 254-nm germicidal UVC [REF]. Murine studies have reported that 222-nm irradiation at doses within the range tested in the present study did not produce detectable corneal damage, whereas equivalent doses of 254-nm UVC caused significant photokeratitis [REF]. The key biophysical rationale -- that 222-nm photons are absorbed within the superficial non-living layers of the tear film and corneal epithelium rather than penetrating to the corneal stroma or endothelium -- is supported by optical modeling studies [REF]. The preservation of CECD observed in the present study is consistent with this absorption model, as endothelial cells lie well below the expected penetration depth of 222-nm photons.

Prior human studies of far-UVC ocular safety have been limited. Previous investigations have largely focused on skin endpoints, with fewer reporting comprehensive ocular assessments [REF]. The current study extends the existing human data by evaluating multiple ocular endpoints simultaneously (VA, refraction, corneal epithelial integrity, endothelial cell density, conjunctival status, and long-term sequelae) over six months. The doses tested here (up to 75 mJ/cm2) substantially exceed current occupational exposure guidelines, which typically set TLVs for 222-nm radiation at approximately 22-23 mJ/cm2 per 8-hour period [REF]. The absence of lasting damage even at approximately three times the TLV provides preliminary evidence that current guidelines may incorporate a meaningful safety margin, though this interpretation requires confirmation in larger studies.

### Interpretation of Transient Corneal Findings

The transient corneal erosion observed at 75 mJ/cm2 in two subjects merits consideration. The mild erosion scores (1 out of a maximum of 3 for both area and density) and rapid resolution (by 1 month) suggest superficial epithelial disruption without involvement of deeper corneal layers. This is consistent with the expected mechanism of 222-nm absorption: energy deposition in the outermost epithelial cells may cause minor cellular damage that is rapidly repaired through normal epithelial turnover, which has a cycle time of approximately 5-7 days [REF]. The absence of erosion at lower doses (22 and 50 mJ/cm2) and its occurrence only at 75 mJ/cm2 suggests a dose-response relationship for acute epithelial effects. Similarly, the dose-dependent increase in subjective symptom severity and duration -- from epiphora sensation alone at 22 mJ/cm2, to discomfort and photophobia at 50 mJ/cm2, to pain and longer-lasting symptoms at 75 mJ/cm2 -- supports a graded biological response. Symptom onset at 2-3 hours is consistent with the time course of UVC-induced corneal epithelial effects described in prior literature for conventional UVC wavelengths [REF], although the symptoms here were substantially milder than those typically reported for 254-nm photokeratitis.

### Strengths and Limitations

The study has several methodological strengths. The use of the contralateral eye as an internal control eliminated between-subject variability in baseline ocular parameters and permitted direct within-subject comparison of irradiated and unirradiated eyes. The stepwise dose-escalation design prioritized subject safety by ensuring that higher doses were only administered after confirming the absence of significant damage at lower doses. The 222-nm source was well-characterized spectrally, with low spectral impurity (0.55%), minimizing the contribution of longer-wavelength UVC that would confound the assessment of 222-nm-specific effects. The six-month follow-up period, while not capturing potential very long-term effects, was sufficient to detect persistent or progressive corneal, lenticular, or conjunctival pathology. Monitoring of subjects during irradiation via two cameras ensured consistent exposure conditions.

Several limitations should be acknowledged. The sample size of five subjects limits statistical power and the ability to detect rare adverse events. The dose-escalation design, while ethically appropriate, resulted in unequal group sizes (n=1 for 22 mJ/cm2, n=2 each for 50 and 75 mJ/cm2), preventing formal dose-response statistical modeling. The study did not assess tear film biomarkers, inflammatory cytokines, or corneal nerve function, which may have provided additional mechanistic insight into the transient epithelial effects observed at 75 mJ/cm2. The six-month follow-up period may be insufficient to detect slowly developing conditions such as climatic droplet keratopathy or pterygium, which may require years of cumulative exposure. Whether repeated daily exposures at these dose levels, as would occur in occupational settings, produce cumulative effects not captured by single-exposure studies remains unknown. The irradiation was performed in a controlled laboratory setting; real-world far-UVC installations involve variable distances, angles, and durations of exposure that could alter the effective ocular dose.

### Clinical Implications

The findings of this study have direct relevance for the expanding deployment of far-UVC germicidal systems in public and healthcare environments. The absence of lasting ocular damage at doses up to 75 mJ/cm2 -- approximately three times the current TLV -- provides initial reassurance regarding the safety margins of 222-nm far-UVC technology for incidental ocular exposure. Clinicians involved in occupational health assessments for workers in environments with far-UVC installations should be aware that transient ocular symptoms (epiphora, discomfort, mild pain) may occur with higher-dose exposures but appear self-limiting. The transient corneal erosion observed at 75 mJ/cm2 suggests that this dose level approaches the threshold for acute epithelial effects, and facilities deploying far-UVC systems should aim to maintain cumulative ocular doses well below this level through appropriate engineering controls (distance, shielding, exposure time limits). Ophthalmologists evaluating patients with far-UVC exposure should include fluorescein staining to assess corneal epithelial integrity, as standard VA testing alone may not capture subclinical epithelial effects.

## Conclusions

This prospective, interventional study demonstrated that single-session 222-nm far-UVC irradiation at doses of 22, 50, and 75 mJ/cm2 did not produce lasting ocular damage in five healthy human subjects over a six-month follow-up period. BCVA, SERE, CECD, and conjunctival hyperemia remained stable in both irradiated and control eyes. Transient, self-limited corneal epithelial erosion and subjective ocular symptoms occurred at the highest dose (75 mJ/cm2), with a dose-dependent pattern observed for symptom severity and duration. Clinicians and occupational health professionals overseeing environments equipped with 222-nm far-UVC germicidal systems should ensure that engineering controls maintain cumulative ocular doses below 75 mJ/cm2, and should include slit lamp examination with fluorescein staining in the evaluation of any individual reporting ocular symptoms following far-UVC exposure. Larger, multi-center studies with longer follow-up and repeated-exposure protocols are needed to establish definitive safe exposure thresholds and to evaluate cumulative effects relevant to occupational and public health deployment of this technology.
