# Long-Term Progression of Diabetic Retinopathy in a Large US Primary Care Cohort: A Prospective Observational Study Using Standardized Fundus Photography

## Abstract

**Purpose:** Diabetic retinopathy (DR) is a leading cause of vision loss among working-age adults, yet longitudinal data on its natural progression in primary care settings remain limited. The present study aimed to characterize the long-term progression of DR severity, the development of clinically significant macular edema (CSME) and proliferative diabetic retinopathy (PDR), and the influence of race and diabetes mellitus (DM) type on DR worsening in a large US primary care population.

**Methods:** This prospective, longitudinal, non-interventional, observational cohort study analyzed de-identified 7-field color fundus photographs from adults with DM (aged 18 years or older) screened for DR at primary care centers across the United States between January 1999 and December 2016. DR severity was graded using the Early Treatment Diabetic Retinopathy Study-Diabetic Retinopathy Severity Scale (ETDRS-DRSS) by masked professional graders at a centralized reading center. Kaplan-Meier analyses estimated time to first 2-step or greater DR worsening, development of CSME, PDR, or both.

**Results:** Of 73,321 patients with DM, 22,109 patients (41,977 eyes) met inclusion criteria. The proportion of eyes with 2-step or greater DR worsening was 2.7% at year 2, 7.1% at year 4, and 9.6% at year 5. Eyes with moderate-to-severe nonproliferative DR (NPDR; ETDRS-DRSS levels 43-53) at baseline had 2-step or greater worsening rates of 11.6% at year 2 and 36.4% at year 5. Three distinct clinical phenotypes of vision-threatening DR were observed: progression to CSME alone (1.24% at year 5), PDR alone (0.49%), or both (0.10%). DR worsening rates were higher in Black and White patients compared with Native American patients, and in type 1 versus type 2 DM.

**Conclusions:** In this large primary care cohort, DR progression followed distinct clinical phenotypes that were not dependent on baseline severity, with notable differences by race and DM type. These findings may inform risk stratification and screening strategies in primary care.

## Introduction

Diabetic retinopathy is the most common microvascular complication of diabetes mellitus and remains a leading cause of preventable blindness worldwide [1,2]. The global prevalence of DM continues to rise, and with it the burden of DR-related visual impairment [3,4]. DR progresses through a spectrum of severity, from mild nonproliferative changes to sight-threatening stages including proliferative diabetic retinopathy and diabetic macular edema [5,6]. Early detection and timely referral are critical to preventing irreversible vision loss, making DR screening programs an essential component of diabetes care [7,8].

Despite considerable advances in the understanding of DR pathophysiology and treatment, longitudinal data characterizing the natural history of DR progression in real-world primary care settings remain sparse. Most landmark studies on DR progression, including the Early Treatment Diabetic Retinopathy Study and the Wisconsin Epidemiologic Study of Diabetic Retinopathy, were conducted in highly selected clinical trial or epidemiologic cohorts that may not fully represent the broader population of patients with DM managed in primary care [9,10,11]. Furthermore, the extent to which patient-level factors such as race and DM type influence the rate and pattern of DR progression in routine clinical practice is not well established [12,13]. There is growing recognition that DR may not follow a single uniform trajectory but instead may manifest through distinct clinical phenotypes of vision-threatening disease, including pathways leading preferentially to macular edema, proliferative disease, or both [14,15]. However, large-scale longitudinal evidence supporting this concept from community-based populations is limited.

A clearer understanding of DR progression patterns in primary care, where the majority of DR screening occurs, is needed to guide clinical decision-making regarding screening intervals and referral thresholds. The aim of the present study was to characterize the long-term progression of DR severity using standardized ETDRS-DRSS grading in a large, geographically diverse US primary care cohort. Specifically, the study sought to estimate the cumulative incidence of 2-step or greater DR worsening over a 5-year period, to evaluate the development of CSME and PDR as distinct outcomes, and to examine the influence of race and DM type on DR progression rates.

## Methods

### Study Design and Setting

This was a prospective, longitudinal, non-interventional, observational cohort study conducted using a historical database of 7-field color fundus photograph images collected from primary care centers across the United States. De-identified data were acquired by a pharmaceutical company from a retinal imaging company that had previously provided DR monitoring services in the DM primary care setting. The study was approved by a university health sciences center institutional review board (IRB), and all participants provided written informed consent prior to capture of photographs.

### Study Population

The study population comprised adults aged 18 years or older with DM who underwent DR screening in either eye between January 1999 and December 2016. From a total database of 73,321 unique patients with DM, only patients with fundus images associated with 2 or more valid ETDRS-DRSS assessments in either eye were included. A total of 51,212 patients were excluded because they had no images or no gradable images at 2 or more visits, yielding a final study population of 22,109 patients (41,977 eyes).

### Image Acquisition and Grading

Seven-field color fundus photographs were obtained at each screening visit. All images were graded by professional graders at a centralized reading center located at a university medical center. Images were double graded by readers who were masked to each other's results. In cases of disagreement, an independent third reader performed adjudication. Due to the historical nature of the database, optical coherence tomography (OCT) images were not available, and patient data were limited to age, sex, race, and diabetes type.

### DR Severity Classification

DR severity was classified according to the ETDRS-DRSS as follows: absent (level 10), mild microaneurysm only (level 20), mild NPDR (level 35), moderate NPDR (level 43), moderately severe NPDR (level 47), severe NPDR (level 53), mild PDR (level 61), moderate PDR (level 65), high-risk PDR (level 71/75), and advanced PDR (level 81/85).

### Definition of CSME

Clinically significant macular edema was defined according to ETDRS criteria as retinal thickening and/or hard exudates adjacent to thickening occurring at or within 500 micrometers of the fovea.

### Outcomes

The primary outcomes were: (1) 2-step or greater DR worsening, defined as a 2-step or greater increase on the ETDRS-DRSS compared with the initial assessment; (2) development of CSME; (3) development of PDR; and (4) development of CSME and PDR together. Prespecified subanalyses assessed the impact of race and DM type on time to first 2-step or greater DR worsening.

### Statistical Analysis

Time-to-event distributions for the primary outcomes (time to first 2-step or greater DR worsening, development of CSME, PDR, or CSME and PDR) were estimated using the Kaplan-Meier method. Time to first 2-step or greater DR worsening was summarized by initial DRSS score. Patients who had color fundus photographs evaluable for the DRSS score at 2 or more visits in either eye were included. The number of eyes at risk of developing DR at year N referred to the number of eyes from patients who had N or more years of follow-up data available.

## Results

### Patient Characteristics

Between January 1999 and December 2016, the database collected images and data from 73,321 unique patients with DM. After exclusion of 51,212 patients who had no images or no gradable images at 2 or more visits, the remaining 22,109 patients (41,977 eyes) constituted the study population (Fig. 1). Baseline patient demographics are presented in Table 1. The median age was 61 years (range 6-85 years), and among patients with known sex, 83.0% were male (n = 14,021) and 17.0% were female (n = 2,876); sex was unknown for 5,212 patients. Only a subset of patients (n = 4,085) reported their race or ethnicity; among these, 48.0% were White (n = 1,948), 41.0% were Native American (n = 1,662), 9.1% were African American/Black (n = 369), 1.2% were Hispanic (n = 50), and 0.7% were of other racial/ethnic backgrounds (n = 29). Race was unknown for 18,051 patients. Only a subset of patients (n = 11,334) reported DM type; among those, 94.2% had type 2 DM (n = 10,676), 5.8% had type 1 DM (n = 653), and fewer than 0.1% had gestational DM (n = 5). DM type was unknown for 10,775 patients.

At baseline, the majority of eyes had no DR (ETDRS-DRSS level 10; 76.7%, n = 32,219). Eyes with ETDRS-DRSS level 14 comprised 2.9% (n = 1,234), level 15 comprised 4.0% (n = 1,687), level 20 comprised 5.8% (n = 2,420), and level 35 comprised 7.5% (n = 3,166). Eyes with moderate NPDR (level 43) comprised 1.5% (n = 639), moderately severe NPDR (level 47) comprised 0.6% (n = 266), and severe NPDR (level 53) comprised 0.3% (n = 104). Eyes with PDR at baseline were uncommon: mild PDR (level 61) comprised 0.3% (n = 138), moderate PDR (level 65) comprised 0.2% (n = 87), high-risk PDR (level 71) comprised 0.1% (n = 31), high-risk PDR (level 75) comprised less than 0.1% (n = 5), and advanced PDR (level 81) comprised less than 0.1% (n = 1).

### Progression of DR by ETDRS-DRSS

For all eyes evaluated, the Kaplan-Meier estimated proportion of eyes with 2-step or greater DR worsening was 2.7% at year 2, 7.1% at year 4, and 9.6% at year 5 (Fig. 2). The percentage of eyes with 2-step or greater DR worsening after year 2 was greatest among eyes with moderate-to-severe NPDR (ETDRS-DRSS levels 43-53) at baseline (Fig. 3). For pooled moderate-to-severe NPDR (levels 43-53) eyes at baseline (999 patients, 1,357 eyes), the proportion of eyes with 2-step or greater worsening was 11.6% at year 2, 26.4% at year 4, and 36.4% at year 5 (Fig. 4). Similar findings were reported among eyes with moderately severe to severe NPDR (levels 47-53) at baseline.

### Development of CSME, PDR, or Both

Among all available eyes with no CSME and no PDR (ETDRS-DRSS levels 10-53) at baseline (21,757 patients, 41,272 eyes), three patterns of progression to vision-threatening DR were observed. The Kaplan-Meier estimated proportion progressing to CSME alone was 1.24% at year 5, the proportion progressing to PDR alone was 0.49% at year 5, and the proportion progressing to both CSME and PDR was 0.10% at year 5 (Fig. 5a). Among eyes with no CSME and moderate-to-severe NPDR (ETDRS-DRSS levels 43-53) at baseline (810 patients, 1,046 eyes), the same three patterns of progression were observed, with higher rates compared with the overall population (Fig. 5b). Among eyes with no CSME and moderately severe to severe NPDR (ETDRS-DRSS levels 47-53) at baseline (265 patients, 310 eyes), similar patterns were again observed (Fig. 5c). Patients with moderate-to-severe NPDR at baseline had higher rates of progression to PDR or CSME compared with the overall study population.

### Subanalysis by Race

In the subanalysis of all eyes categorized by race, the rate of 2-step or greater worsening was higher in Black and White patients compared with Native American patients. By year 2, the proportion of eyes with 2-step or greater worsening was 7.6% in Black patients, 4.8% in White patients, and 1.6% in Native American patients (Fig. 6).

### Subanalysis by DM Type

In the subanalysis of all eyes categorized by DM type, the estimated proportion of DR worsening by 2 steps or greater was higher with type 1 DM compared with type 2 DM (Fig. 7).

## Discussion

The present study characterized the long-term progression of DR in a large, real-world US primary care cohort of 22,109 patients (41,977 eyes) followed over a period of up to 5 years. Using standardized 7-field fundus photography with centralized ETDRS-DRSS grading, the study found that overall rates of 2-step or greater DR worsening were relatively low (9.6% at year 5), while eyes with moderate-to-severe NPDR at baseline experienced substantially higher progression rates (36.4% at year 5). A key observation was the identification of three distinct clinical phenotypes of vision-threatening DR progression -- CSME alone, PDR alone, and both CSME and PDR -- that appeared to be independent of baseline DR severity. Additionally, differences in DR progression rates were observed by race and DM type.

### DR Progression Rates in Primary Care

The overall 5-year rate of 2-step or greater DR worsening of 9.6% observed in the present study provides important context for DR screening in primary care. This rate is generally consistent with prior estimates from population-based studies, although direct comparisons are complicated by differences in study populations, DR grading methods, and follow-up durations [10,16,17]. The relatively low overall progression rate likely reflects the predominance of eyes with no DR or mild DR at baseline (76.7% had ETDRS-DRSS level 10), consistent with the early detection setting of primary care screening. These findings may support the concept that patients with absent or minimal DR at baseline are at relatively low short-term risk of clinically meaningful worsening, which has implications for the optimization of screening intervals [18,19].

### Progression in Moderate-to-Severe NPDR

Eyes with moderate-to-severe NPDR (ETDRS-DRSS levels 43-53) at baseline had markedly higher rates of DR worsening, with 11.6% progressing by 2 or more steps at year 2 and 36.4% at year 5. This finding aligns with established evidence that the risk of progression to sight-threatening DR increases substantially at higher baseline severity levels [9,20,21]. The approximately four-fold higher 2-year progression rate in moderate-to-severe NPDR eyes (11.6%) compared with all eyes (2.7%) underscores the clinical importance of identifying these patients for closer monitoring and potential early intervention. These data may support current clinical guidelines recommending more frequent follow-up for patients with moderate or worse NPDR [7,22].

### Distinct Clinical Phenotypes of Vision-Threatening DR

A notable finding of the present study was the observation of three distinct clinical phenotypes of progression to vision-threatening DR: progression to CSME alone, to PDR alone, or to both CSME and PDR. Among all eyes without CSME or PDR at baseline, the 5-year rate of progression to CSME alone (1.24%) was approximately 2.5 times higher than progression to PDR alone (0.49%), while progression to both was uncommon (0.10%). These patterns were consistent across subgroups stratified by baseline DR severity, suggesting that the pathways leading to macular edema versus proliferative disease may be at least partially independent biological processes rather than simply reflecting differences in baseline severity. This observation is consistent with emerging evidence from clinical and pathophysiological studies suggesting that diabetic macular edema and PDR may involve distinct, though overlapping, mechanisms of retinal vascular damage [14,15,23,24]. If confirmed in additional studies, these findings could have implications for the development of targeted therapeutic approaches and personalized risk assessment strategies.

### Racial Differences in DR Progression

The subanalysis by race revealed higher rates of 2-step or greater DR worsening in Black patients (7.6% at year 2) and White patients (4.8% at year 2) compared with Native American patients (1.6% at year 2). These differences may reflect a combination of biological, socioeconomic, and healthcare access factors that are known to influence DR outcomes across racial and ethnic groups [12,25]. Previous studies have reported higher prevalence and severity of DR among Black individuals compared with White individuals in some populations [13,26], although the finding of lower progression rates in Native American patients in the present study is less well characterized in the existing literature. It is important to note that only a small subset of patients (n = 4,085) in the present study reported race, and the results of this subanalysis should be interpreted with caution given the potential for selection bias. Additionally, important confounders such as glycemic control, blood pressure, and duration of diabetes were not available in this dataset and may contribute to observed racial differences.

### Influence of DM Type

The higher rate of DR worsening observed in type 1 DM compared with type 2 DM is consistent with prior reports indicating that patients with type 1 DM may be at greater risk of DR progression [10,27,28]. This finding may reflect differences in the pathophysiology, duration, and metabolic characteristics of type 1 versus type 2 DM. However, as with the race subanalysis, information on DM type was available for only a subset of patients (n = 11,334), limiting the generalizability of this finding.

### Strengths

The present study utilized a large cohort of 22,109 patients with over 41,000 eyes followed longitudinally over a period of up to 5 years in a real-world primary care setting. DR severity was assessed using standardized 7-field color fundus photography graded according to the ETDRS-DRSS at a centralized reading center by professional graders using a double-grading protocol with independent adjudication. The study included patients from primary care centers across the United States, providing geographic diversity. The use of Kaplan-Meier time-to-event analyses allowed estimation of cumulative progression rates while accounting for varying follow-up durations. The analysis of CSME and PDR as separate and combined endpoints enabled the identification of distinct clinical progression phenotypes.

### Limitations

Several limitations of the present study should be acknowledged. First, due to the historical nature of the database, clinical data were limited to age, sex, race, and DM type; important confounders such as glycemic control (HbA1c), duration of diabetes, blood pressure, lipid levels, and treatment history were not available. Second, OCT imaging was not available, and CSME was assessed based on fundus photography alone according to ETDRS criteria, which may have underestimated the prevalence of diabetic macular edema compared with OCT-based definitions. Third, a large proportion of patients had missing data for race (81.6%) and DM type (48.7%), which limits the interpretation and generalizability of the race and DM type subanalyses. Fourth, 51,212 patients (69.8%) were excluded from the original database because they did not have gradable images at 2 or more visits, which could introduce selection bias if excluded patients differed systematically from included patients. Fifth, information on patient management before and after imaging was not available, meaning that treatment effects (such as laser photocoagulation or anti-vascular endothelial growth factor therapy) could have influenced observed progression rates. Sixth, the predominance of male patients (83.0%) in the study population limits the generalizability of findings to female patients with DM. Finally, the study was limited to a maximum follow-up of 5 years; longer-term follow-up would be needed to characterize DR progression over the full course of the disease.

### Conclusions

In this large, prospective, longitudinal observational study of DR progression in a US primary care setting, overall rates of 2-step or greater DR worsening over 5 years were relatively low, while eyes with moderate-to-severe NPDR at baseline experienced substantially higher progression rates. The identification of three distinct clinical phenotypes of vision-threatening DR progression -- CSME alone, PDR alone, and both -- that appeared independent of baseline severity suggests that different pathogenic mechanisms may drive these outcomes. Differences in DR progression by race and DM type were also observed, although these subanalyses were limited by incomplete data. These findings may help inform DR screening strategies and risk stratification in primary care, and they highlight the need for further research into the distinct pathways of vision-threatening DR progression.
