# Draft Metadata: Writing Decisions and Assumptions

## General Approach

- The paper was written entirely from the extracted data bundle without access to the original manuscript.
- IMRAD structure was followed throughout, with a structured abstract, introduction, methods, results, discussion (including strengths, limitations, and conclusions subsections).
- The working title was crafted to be descriptive of the study content: large US primary care cohort, DR progression, prospective observational design, standardized fundus photography.

## Title Decision

- The title emphasizes the key study features: long-term progression, large cohort, US primary care setting, and prospective observational design with standardized fundus photography.
- No attempt was made to identify or replicate any original title.

## Abstract

- Structured with Purpose, Methods, Results, and Conclusions subheadings.
- Word count is approximately 240 words, within the 200-250 word target.
- Key quantitative findings from all major analyses were included.

## Introduction

- Three paragraphs following background-gap-objective structure.
- Paragraph 1: General background on DR burden and importance of screening.
- Paragraph 2: Gap in knowledge — limited real-world longitudinal data from primary care, limited understanding of distinct phenotypes and demographic influences.
- Paragraph 3: Study aims stated using observational study language ("aimed to characterize," "sought to estimate").
- All general clinical facts were hedged and marked with [REF] placeholders.
- No hypothesis language was used; aim/objective language was used throughout.

## Methods

- All subsections from the extracted data were preserved: Study Design and Setting, Study Population, Image Acquisition and Grading, DR Severity Classification, Definition of CSME, Outcomes, Statistical Analysis.
- Past tense used throughout.
- All specific definitions (ETDRS-DRSS levels, CSME criteria) were reported faithfully.
- The number at risk definition from the extracted data was incorporated into the Statistical Analysis section.

## Results

- Strictly descriptive language was used — no interpretive phrases such as "indicating that," "suggesting that," or "demonstrating that."
- All quantitative data were reported with exact values as provided in the extracted data.
- Results were organized into the same subsections as in the extracted data: Patient Characteristics, Progression by ETDRS-DRSS, Development of CSME/PDR/Both, Subanalysis by Race, Subanalysis by DM Type.
- Past tense used throughout.

## Discussion

- Five thematic paragraphs plus dedicated Strengths, Limitations, and Conclusions subsections.
- Paragraph 1: Overall summary of key findings.
- Paragraph 2: DR progression rates in primary care context, comparison with prior literature.
- Paragraph 3: Moderate-to-severe NPDR progression rates and clinical significance.
- Paragraph 4: Distinct clinical phenotypes of vision-threatening DR — the central novel finding.
- Paragraph 5: Racial differences in DR progression.
- Additional paragraph: DM type differences.
- Hedging language used consistently ("may," "suggests," "appears to").
- [REF] placeholders used where citations to prior literature would be expected.

## Assumptions and Uncertainties

1. **Eye count discrepancy**: The extracted data noted a discrepancy between 41,977 and 41,997 eyes reported in different locations. I used 41,977 for the main text and Table 1 (as in the study design and Table 1), and 41,997 where the original figure captions used that number. This was noted in the figure legends.

2. **Sex distribution**: The male predominance (83.0%) is unusual for a diabetes cohort. This may reflect the specific data source (e.g., Veterans Affairs or military-associated primary care centers). I noted this as a limitation but did not speculate on the source in the main text.

3. **Missing data**: Very large proportions of missing data for race (81.6%) and DM type (48.7%) were noted and explicitly discussed as limitations.

4. **ETDRS-DRSS levels 14 and 15**: These levels appeared in the baseline distribution table but were not included in the severity classification list provided in the study design. These levels typically represent "questionable" DR changes. They were included in the results table but not specifically defined in the methods, following the extracted data.

5. **Specific percentages for DM type subanalysis**: The extracted data noted that specific percentage values were not provided for the DM type subanalysis (Fig. 7). The results text therefore describes the direction of the finding only, consistent with the available data.

6. **No p-values**: The extracted data contained no formal statistical comparisons or p-values. The Kaplan-Meier analyses were descriptive. Results were reported without statistical comparison language.

7. **Treatment effects**: The study could not account for treatments received during follow-up (e.g., laser, anti-VEGF), as management data were not available. This was discussed as a limitation.
