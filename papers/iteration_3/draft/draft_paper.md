# The Association between Diabetic Retinopathy and Macular Degeneration: A Nationwide Population-Based Study

---

## Abstract

**Purpose:** Age-related macular degeneration (AMD), particularly its exudative form, is a leading cause of vision impairment in older adults. As diabetes becomes increasingly prevalent in aging populations, it is important to investigate the potential relationship between diabetic retinopathy (DR) and AMD. The present study aimed to assess the risk of developing overall, non-exudative, and exudative AMD in individuals with DR compared to those without retinopathy (non-DR) using a nationwide population-based cohort in Taiwan.

**Methods:** A retrospective cohort study was conducted using the Taiwan National Health Insurance Database (NHIRD), specifically the Longitudinal Health Insurance Database (LHID) 2000, covering the period from 2000 to 2013. Patients aged 55 years or older who were newly diagnosed with DR (International Classification of Diseases, Ninth Revision, Clinical Modification [ICD-9-CM] codes 362.01 and 362.02) between 2000 and 2006 were identified as the study cohort (N = 3,413). For each DR patient, four age- and sex-matched controls without DR were selected (N = 13,652). Kaplan-Meier analysis and Cox proportional hazards models were used to calculate hazard ratios (HRs) and adjusted hazard ratios (aHRs) for the development of AMD, adjusting for age, sex, low income, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, coronary artery disease (CAD), stroke, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and liver cirrhosis/chronic hepatitis.

**Results:** The DR group had a significantly higher incidence rate of overall AMD (16.77 per 10,000 person-years) compared to the non-DR group (5.36 per 10,000 person-years), with an aHR of 3.50 (95% CI: 3.10-3.95). For senile (unspecified) AMD, the aHR was 3.45 (95% CI: 3.04-3.92). For non-exudative AMD, the aHR was 2.92 (95% CI: 2.08-4.09). For exudative AMD, the aHR was 3.92 (95% CI: 2.51-6.14). Kaplan-Meier analysis demonstrated significantly higher cumulative AMD incidence in the DR group (log-rank test, P < 0.001).

**Conclusions:** DR was a significant risk factor for overall, senile, non-exudative, and exudative AMD, even after adjusting for demographic and comorbid conditions. Early detection and appropriate management of AMD is critically important among DR patients.

**Keywords:** diabetes mellitus; diabetic retinopathy; age-related macular degeneration; Taiwan National Health Insurance Database; retrospective cohort study

---

## 1. Introduction

Age-related macular degeneration (AMD) is a progressive retinal degenerative disorder and a primary cause of irreversible central vision loss in older adults worldwide. The condition encompasses a spectrum of clinical presentations, ranging from the non-exudative (dry) form characterized by drusen and geographic atrophy to the exudative (wet) form marked by choroidal neovascularization and subretinal fluid accumulation. As global populations continue to age, AMD represents an increasingly significant public health burden.

Diabetic retinopathy (DR) is among the most prevalent microvascular complications of diabetes mellitus (DM) and constitutes a leading cause of preventable blindness in working-age and elderly populations. DR and AMD are both important retinal degenerative disorders that represent a growing concern among aging societies. These conditions exhibit overlapping pathological processes, including retinal edema and progressive inflammation within the central macula. Shared pathogenic mechanisms -- such as chronic low-grade inflammation, oxidative stress, and vascular dysfunction -- suggest a potential biological link between these two conditions.

As diabetes becomes increasingly prevalent in aging populations, it is crucial to explore the potential relationship between DR and AMD. Although several studies have examined the association between diabetes and AMD, the results have been inconsistent. Some investigations have reported a positive association, whereas others have found no significant relationship or even a protective effect of diabetes against AMD development. Notably, fewer studies have specifically examined the risk of AMD in individuals with established DR, as opposed to diabetes in general.

The present study aimed to assess the risk of developing overall, non-exudative, and exudative AMD in individuals with DR compared to those without retinopathy (non-DR) using a nationwide population-based retrospective cohort study in Taiwan.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1 Data Source

This study utilized data from the Taiwan National Health Insurance Database (NHIRD). Taiwan's National Health Insurance (NHI) program was launched by the Taiwan Department of Health in 1995 and provides comprehensive medical care coverage. A total of 23,832,551 Taiwanese residents, accounting for approximately 99% of the population in Taiwan, have enrolled in the program. The Longitudinal Health Insurance Database (LHID) 2000, which is part of the NHI Research Database, contains claims data for one million randomly sampled patients from the Registry for Beneficiaries of the NHIRD. The LHID exhibits no statistically significant differences in terms of age, sex, or healthcare costs when compared to all NHI enrollees, supporting its representativeness of the general Taiwanese population.

Diagnoses in the database were assigned by qualified clinical physicians based on laboratory, imaging, and pathological data, following the International Classification of Diseases, Ninth Revision, Clinical Modification (ICD-9-CM). Personal identification numbers were encrypted to safeguard patient privacy before the electronic files were released for research purposes.

### 2.2 Ethics Statement

The study was approved by the Institutional Review Board of Fu Jen Catholic University (FJU-IRB NO: C104014). Informed consent was waived as this study was a retrospective cohort study conducted using de-identified data from the NHIRD.

### 2.3 Study Design and Participants

A retrospective population-based cohort study design was employed. Patients aged 55 years or older who were newly diagnosed with diabetic retinopathy (DR) between 2000 and 2006 were identified as the study cohort using ICD-9-CM codes 362.01 (background diabetic retinopathy) and 362.02 (proliferative diabetic retinopathy). To ensure inclusion of only incident cases, individuals who had received any DR or diabetes mellitus (DM; ICD-9-CM code 250) diagnoses in the medical claims data before the year 2000 were excluded.

For each patient diagnosed with DR, four patients without diabetic retinopathy (non-DR) were randomly selected from the same database as the control group, matched by age and sex. The non-DR group excluded individuals with a history of AMD prior to the index date. The final analytic sample comprised 3,413 subjects with DR (study group) and 13,652 subjects without DR (control group), yielding a matching ratio of 1:4.

### 2.4 Outcome Definition

The primary outcome was the development of AMD, classified according to ICD-9-CM codes. Following the ICD-9-CM format, AMD encompasses several subtypes, including senile (unspecified), non-exudative, exudative, familial, juvenile, congenital, and cystic forms. For the purposes of this study, the three most commonly cited codes by ophthalmologists were selected: senile (unspecified) AMD (ICD-9-CM code 362.50), non-exudative AMD (ICD-9-CM code 362.51), and exudative AMD (ICD-9-CM code 362.52). The combined occurrence of these three codes represented overall AMD. Each AMD subtype was also analyzed separately.

All subjects were observed from the index date until a diagnosis of AMD, death, or 31 December 2013, whichever occurred first.

### 2.5 Covariates

The following comorbid conditions were included as covariates in the adjusted models: age, sex, low income status, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, coronary artery disease (CAD), stroke, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and liver cirrhosis/chronic hepatitis.

### 2.6 Statistical Analysis

Baseline demographic and clinical characteristics were compared between the DR and non-DR groups. Chi-square tests were used to analyze categorical variables, including demographics and comorbidities, while continuous variables were assessed using two-sample t-tests. Kaplan-Meier curves were constructed to depict the cumulative incidence of AMD, with differences between groups assessed using the log-rank test. Cox proportional hazards models were used to estimate hazard ratios (HRs) and adjusted hazard ratios (aHRs) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs) for the risk of AMD development, adjusting for the covariates specified above. Analyses were further stratified by age group and sex to examine the consistency of the DR-AMD association across subpopulations.

All statistical analyses were performed using SAS version 9.4 (SAS Institute Inc., Cary, NC, USA) and SPSS version 22.0 (IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA). A two-tailed P-value of less than 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Baseline Characteristics

The baseline demographic information and comorbidity conditions of patients in the DR group (N = 3,413) and the non-DR group (N = 13,652) are presented in Table 1. The mean age was 67.1 years in the DR group and 66.5 years in the non-DR group (P < 0.001). Age group distribution and sex distribution were well-balanced between groups following the matching procedure (P = 0.999 for both).

The DR group had significantly higher prevalence of all assessed comorbidities compared to the non-DR group, including hypertension, hyperlipidemia, coronary artery disease, stroke, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, and liver cirrhosis/chronic hepatitis (all P < 0.001). Low income status was also significantly more prevalent in the DR group (P < 0.001).

### 3.2 Incidence and Hazard Ratios of Overall AMD

Table 2 presents the incidence rates and estimated hazard ratios for all types of AMD combined (ICD-9-CM codes 362.50, 362.51, and 362.52). The incidence rate of overall AMD was 16.77 per 10,000 person-years in the DR group compared to 5.36 per 10,000 person-years in the non-DR group. After adjustment for age, sex, low income, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, CAD, stroke, COPD, and liver cirrhosis/chronic hepatitis, the DR group had a significantly elevated risk of overall AMD (aHR = 3.50; 95% CI: 3.10-3.95).

In stratified analyses, the incidence of AMD increased with age in both groups; however, the association was stronger in the DR group across all age categories, with the highest risk observed in patients over 85 years of age. The incidence rates of AMD were higher for both sexes in the DR group, with male patients showing a slightly higher risk than female patients. The DR group had a significantly elevated HR and aHR compared to the non-DR group regardless of the presence or absence of any comorbid condition, including hypertension, CAD, hyperlipidemia, stroke, COPD, and liver cirrhosis/chronic hepatitis.

### 3.3 Incidence and Hazard Ratios of Senile (Unspecified) AMD

Table 3 presents the incidence and estimated hazard ratios of senile (unspecified) AMD (ICD-9-CM code 362.50). The incidence rate was 14.65 per 10,000 person-years in the DR group compared to 4.78 per 10,000 person-years in the non-DR group. The adjusted hazard ratio was 3.45 (95% CI: 3.04-3.92).

In the DR group, the incidence rate of senile AMD was markedly higher with advancing age, particularly in the 65-75 and 85 years and older age groups. The DR group had significantly elevated risk of senile AMD independent of demographic factors and the presence or absence of comorbid conditions.

### 3.4 Incidence and Hazard Ratios of Non-Exudative AMD

Table 4 presents the incidence and estimated hazard ratios of non-exudative AMD (ICD-9-CM code 362.51). The adjusted hazard ratio was 2.92 (95% CI: 2.08-4.09). Male patients with DR had a higher incidence rate of non-exudative AMD compared to female patients with DR.

### 3.5 Incidence and Hazard Ratios of Exudative AMD

Table 5 presents the incidence and estimated hazard ratios of exudative AMD (ICD-9-CM code 362.52). The incidence rate of exudative AMD was 1.37 per 10,000 person-years in the DR group compared to 0.31 per 10,000 person-years in the non-DR group. The adjusted hazard ratio was 3.92 (95% CI: 2.51-6.14).

After adjustment for confounding factors, the hazard ratios of exudative AMD remained significantly elevated in the DR group. The risk of exudative AMD was influenced by the presence of cardiovascular comorbid conditions, including hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and CAD.

### 3.6 Kaplan-Meier Analysis

Kaplan-Meier survival analysis demonstrated a significantly higher cumulative incidence of AMD in the DR group compared to the non-DR group (log-rank test, P < 0.001). The cumulative incidence curves for all AMD subtypes diverged progressively over the follow-up period, with the DR group consistently showing higher cumulative AMD risk. Kaplan-Meier curves for overall AMD (Figure 2), senile (unspecified) AMD (Figure 3), non-exudative AMD (Figure 4), and exudative AMD (Figure 5) all showed significantly higher cumulative incidence in the DR group.

## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 Summary of Key Findings

The present population-based cohort study explored the risks of developing age-related macular degeneration (AMD) in its senile (unspecified), non-exudative, and exudative forms among elderly patients with diabetic retinopathy (DR) compared to matched controls without DR. The principal finding was that DR was significantly associated with an elevated risk of all AMD subtypes. The adjusted hazard ratios ranged from 2.92 for non-exudative AMD to 3.92 for exudative AMD, with overall AMD carrying an aHR of 3.50 (95% CI: 3.10-3.95). These associations persisted after comprehensive adjustment for demographic factors and multiple comorbid conditions, and remained consistent across age group and sex strata. Kaplan-Meier analysis further confirmed a significantly higher cumulative incidence of AMD in the DR group (log-rank test, P < 0.001), with the cumulative incidence curves diverging progressively over the follow-up period.

### 4.2 Baseline Characteristics and Comorbidity Burden

The initial assessment of baseline characteristics demonstrated that age and sex distributions were similar between the DR and non-DR groups following the matching procedure. However, a notably higher prevalence of comorbidities was observed in the DR group, including hypertension, hyperlipidemia, coronary artery disease, stroke, COPD, and liver cirrhosis/chronic hepatitis (all P < 0.001). These findings are consistent with the well-established association between diabetes and cardiovascular risk factors. Despite the higher comorbidity burden in the DR group, the association between DR and AMD remained significant after adjustment for all of these conditions, suggesting that the ocular pathologies inherent to DR may exert effects on AMD development beyond those attributable to systemic vascular comorbidities.

### 4.3 Shared Pathological Mechanisms

The observed association between DR and AMD is biologically plausible given the shared pathological mechanisms underlying these two retinal conditions. Both DR and AMD involve chronic inflammation, oxidative stress, and vascular dysfunction within the retina. In DR, chronic hyperglycemia leads to microvascular damage, pericyte loss, blood-retinal barrier breakdown, and activation of inflammatory cascades. These pathological processes -- including the upregulation of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), accumulation of advanced glycation end-products, and increased oxidative stress -- may create a retinal microenvironment that is more susceptible to the degenerative processes of AMD. Similarly, retinal edema and progressive inflammation within the central macula are features common to both conditions. Both genetic and non-genetic factors, including aging, hyperglycemia, and hypertension, can disrupt retinal homeostasis and trigger inflammatory cascades leading to vascular dysfunction and retinal cell death.

The finding that exudative AMD had the highest adjusted hazard ratio (aHR = 3.92) is particularly noteworthy, as both exudative AMD and proliferative DR share a common pathogenic feature of neovascularization driven by VEGF upregulation. This shared angiogenic pathway may partly explain the stronger association observed with the exudative AMD subtype.

### 4.4 Comparison with Prior Literature

The results of the present study are consistent with prior investigations that have demonstrated an association between diabetic retinal disease and subsequent AMD development. A previous Taiwan-based study using the LHID examined 71,904 patients with diabetes and 270,213 patients without diabetes aged 50 years or older and found that nonexudative AMD (HR 3.89; P = 0.001) and exudative AMD (HR 3.42; P < 0.001) were significantly correlated with diabetes with DR cohorts compared to diabetes without DR cohorts. That study provided large-scale, population-based evidence that diabetes with retinopathy is independently associated with an increased risk of subsequent AMD development.

The broader literature on the diabetes-AMD relationship has yielded mixed results. While some epidemiological studies have identified diabetes as a risk factor for AMD, others have not found a significant association, and a small number have even suggested a potential protective effect. The inconsistency may be attributable to differences in study design, population characteristics, DR severity classification, AMD diagnostic criteria, and the specific confounders adjusted for in statistical models. The present study's focus specifically on DR rather than diabetes in general, combined with the large sample size and comprehensive covariate adjustment, strengthens the evidence for a specific DR-AMD association.

International studies from various populations, including the Kailuan Eye Study, have also examined the association between DR and AMD prevalence, providing cross-cultural context for the present findings. The consistent direction of the association across different populations and study designs supports the robustness of the DR-AMD link.

### 4.5 Age, Sex, and Comorbidity Interactions

The stratified analyses revealed that the incidence of AMD increased with age in both groups, but the association was stronger in the DR group across all age categories, with the highest risk observed in patients over 85 years of age. This age-dependent pattern is consistent with the progressive nature of both DR and AMD and the cumulative effects of chronic retinal inflammation and oxidative stress over time. Male patients with DR showed a slightly higher risk of AMD compared to female patients, though DR was a significant risk factor for AMD in both sexes.

The DR group had significantly higher AMD risk regardless of the presence or absence of any assessed comorbid condition. The risks of exudative AMD were notably influenced by cardiovascular comorbid conditions such as hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and CAD. These findings suggest that while vascular comorbidities may modulate the DR-AMD association, the intrinsic retinal pathology of DR itself is the primary driver of increased AMD risk.

### 4.6 Clinical Implications

The findings of this study carry important clinical implications. The significantly elevated risk of AMD across all subtypes in DR patients underscores the need for vigilant screening for AMD in patients with established DR, particularly in older adults. Early detection and appropriate treatment of AMD is critically important among DR patients. Given that DR patients already undergo regular ophthalmic examinations for monitoring of retinopathy progression, these visits represent an opportunity for concurrent AMD screening. Clinicians managing DR patients should maintain a high index of suspicion for AMD development and consider incorporating AMD-specific assessments into routine DR surveillance protocols.

### 4.7 Strengths

The present study has several notable strengths. First, it utilized the Taiwan NHIRD, which covers approximately 99% of the Taiwanese population and represents one of the largest and most comprehensive health insurance databases in Asia. The LHID 2000, drawn from this database, exhibits no statistically significant differences in terms of age, sex, or healthcare costs when compared to all NHI enrollees, supporting the generalizability of the findings to the broader Taiwanese population. Second, the large sample size (3,413 DR subjects and 13,652 non-DR controls) provided adequate statistical power to detect associations across AMD subtypes. Third, the matched cohort design with 1:4 matching by age and sex minimized the potential for selection bias and confounding by these key demographic variables. Fourth, the long follow-up period spanning from 2000 to 2013 allowed for adequate observation time for AMD development. Fifth, diagnoses in the database were assigned by qualified clinical physicians based on laboratory, imaging, and pathological data following ICD-9-CM coding. Sixth, the comprehensive adjustment for multiple comorbidities -- including age, sex, low income, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, CAD, stroke, COPD, and liver cirrhosis/chronic hepatitis -- strengthened the validity of the observed associations.

---

## 5. Limitations

This study has several limitations that should be considered when interpreting the findings. First, the study utilized ICD-9-CM coding to identify AMD cases. Following the ICD-9-CM format, AMD has several subtypes (senile/unspecified, non-exudative, exudative, familial, juvenile, congenital, cystic, etc.), and the most commonly cited codes by ophthalmologists -- senile/unspecified (362.50), non-exudative (362.51), and exudative (362.52) -- were selected to represent all types of AMD for convenience of analysis. This design may result in inconsistency of codes cited by ophthalmologists and could lead to misclassification of some AMD cases.

Second, as with all administrative database studies, the NHIRD relies on ICD-9-CM coding by physicians, which may be subject to coding errors or variability in diagnostic practices across clinical settings.

Third, the study was unable to control for certain important unmeasured confounders that may influence AMD risk, including smoking status, body mass index (BMI), genetic factors, dietary intake, nutritional supplement use, and other lifestyle variables.

Fourth, the database does not contain detailed clinical information such as best-corrected visual acuity, optical coherence tomography (OCT) findings, fundus photography details, fluorescein angiography results, severity grading of DR, or specific treatment data (e.g., anti-VEGF injections, laser photocoagulation), which would have provided additional context for the observed associations.

Fifth, surveillance bias represents a potential concern. Patients with DR may undergo more frequent ophthalmologic examinations than individuals without DR, potentially leading to higher detection rates of AMD in the DR group. This detection bias could partly inflate the observed hazard ratios.

---

## 6. Conclusions

Diabetic retinopathy was a significant risk factor for overall, senile (unspecified), non-exudative, and exudative age-related macular degeneration, even after comprehensive adjustment for demographic factors and comorbid conditions. The adjusted hazard ratios ranged from 2.92 (non-exudative AMD) to 3.92 (exudative AMD), with overall AMD at 3.50. DR patients tend to have a higher prevalence of vascular comorbidities; however, the findings indicate that the ocular pathologies inherent to DR may have a more significant impact on the progression to AMD beyond what is attributable to systemic vascular disease. Early detection and appropriate treatment of AMD is critically important among DR patients, and clinicians should consider incorporating AMD-specific screening into routine DR surveillance protocols.
