## Paper: Impact of Ophthalmic Clinical Service Use in Mitigating Myopia Onset and Progression in Preschool Children

## 1. Abstract Comparison

### A. Structure Analysis

| Aspect | Original | Draft |
|--------|----------|-------|
| Sub-sections | Background, Methods, Results, Conclusions | Purpose, Methods, Results, Conclusions |
| Paragraph count | 4 labeled sections | 4 labeled sections |
| Approximate word count | ~200 words | ~350 words |

**Observations:**
- The draft abstract is approximately 75% longer than the original.
- The original uses "Background" while the draft uses "Purpose" -- a minor labeling difference.
- The draft abstract front-loads a general epidemiological framing ("Myopia has become a significant public health concern worldwide") that is absent from the original, which jumps directly into the screening-referral problem.
- The draft includes substantially more quantitative detail in the Results section (HR values, median survival times, SE progression values), whereas the original abstract is deliberately concise, reporting only cumulative incidence percentages.

### B. Content Analysis

**Key points in original but missing or altered in draft:**
- The original names the three specific districts (Jing'an, Minhang, Pudong) and their representativeness -- the draft omits these from the abstract.
- The original explicitly states "geographic diversity and economic disparity" -- the draft omits this.
- The original mentions the initial 14,572 children and that 5,917 needed referral -- the draft gives only the final cohort of 5,511.

**Key points added by draft not in original:**
- The draft adds general myopia prevalence framing in the Purpose section.
- The draft includes HR values, median survival times, and SE progression data in the abstract Results.
- The draft provides explicit subgroup analysis mention ("consistent protective effects across sex and district subgroups").

**Numerical discrepancies:**
- **Tardily group N**: Original = 1,215 (22.05%); Draft = 1,012 (18.36%) -- **INCORRECT in draft**
- **Timely group N**: Original = 112 (2.03%); Draft = 315 (5.72%) -- **INCORRECT in draft**
- The draft fabricates a "high-risk subgroup" N of 4,443 for the abstract, whereas the original reports the total cohort of 5,511 and the High-risk group as N = 2,437.

### C. Style Analysis

- The original abstract is terse and factual, using short declarative sentences.
- The draft abstract reads more like a mini-paper, with explanatory clauses and hedging language.
- The original uses simple vocabulary ("sought clinical services"); the draft uses more elaborate phrasing ("demonstrated a significantly lower hazard of myopia onset").
- The original concludes with a policy-oriented statement ("uplifting the degree of ophthalmic clinical health services"); the draft concludes with a clinical-statistical summary ("45% reduction in the hazard").

### Cross-Cutting Observations

1. **Systematic numerical errors**: The draft consistently reports incorrect group sizes for Tardily (1,012 vs. 1,215) and Timely (315 vs. 112) groups. This propagates through every section.

2. **Prose quality vs. data fidelity trade-off**: The draft is generally well-written with good sentence-level readability, but sacrifices factual precision for fluency.

3. **Over-expansion in Introduction, under-expansion in Discussion**: The draft invests heavily in Introduction context but shortchanges the Discussion, which should be the longest and most nuanced section.

4. **Missing methodological specificity**: Critical protocol details (instruments, formulas, screening procedures) are absent, reducing reproducibility.

5. **Conflation of subgroup data**: The draft mixes up "all children" SE progression values with "high-risk group" values, a substantive analytical error.

6. **Missing standalone sections**: The original has separate Background, Conclusions, Strengths, and Limitations sections. The draft merges these, losing structural clarity.
