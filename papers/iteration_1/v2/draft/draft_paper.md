# Impact of Ophthalmic Clinical Service Use in Mitigating Myopia Onset and Progression in Preschool Children: A Retrospective Cohort Study

---

## Abstract

**Purpose:** Although school vision screenings identify children with vision problems and issue referrals for ophthalmic evaluation, the effectiveness of this approach in mitigating myopia remains unverified. The present study aimed to investigate the impact of ophthalmic clinical service utilization on the onset and progression of myopia in preschool children identified with vision impairment.

**Methods:** This retrospective cohort study utilized data from the Shanghai Child and Adolescent Large-scale Eye Study (SCALE). Children aged 4-6 years from three districts (Jing'an, Minhang, and Pudong) who failed school vision screenings in 2015 were followed through December 2020. Of 14,572 children initially screened, 5,511 who received a referral recommendation met inclusion criteria (two or more screenings, complete personal information). Children were categorized by referral compliance: Never (no clinical visit), Tardily (visit after 3 months), or Timely (visit within 3 months). Cox proportional hazard models estimated hazard ratios (HRs) for myopia incidence in the High-risk group. Generalized additive models (GAM) assessed annual spherical equivalent (SE) progression.

**Results:** Of 5,511 children (mean age, 5.25 years; 52.24% male), 1,327 (24.08%) sought ophthalmic clinical services. After six years, 65.53% developed myopia, with cumulative incidence of 64.76%, 69.31%, and 57.14% in the Never, Tardily, and Timely groups, respectively. The overall incidence rate was 131.06 per 1,000 person-years.

**Conclusions:** Timely utilization of ophthalmic clinical services following school vision screenings was associated with significantly reduced myopia incidence and slower SE progression among preschool children with vision disorders. These findings underscore the importance of promoting referral compliance after school vision screening.

---

## Introduction

Myopia has become a major global public health concern, with prevalence estimates projected to affect nearly half of the world population by 2050 [1]. The myopia epidemic is particularly pronounced in East and Southeast Asia, where prevalence rates among young adults have reached 80-90%, driven primarily by intensive educational demands and limited time spent outdoors [2]. Among the populations at increasing risk, preschool-aged children represent a critical group, as early onset of myopia is associated with a greater likelihood of progression to high myopia and its sight-threatening complications in adulthood.

While myopia is commonly observed among school-aged children, its prevalence has traditionally been considered relatively low in children aged 6 years or younger. However, recent longitudinal studies have identified a higher-than-expected rate of vision disorders among preschool children. International data corroborate this pattern: in the United States, a study of preschoolers participating in the Head Start program reported a notable prevalence of refractive errors across racial and ethnic groups [7], while temporal analyses from Hong Kong documented shifts in the distribution of vision disorders among preschoolers over a decade [8]. In the Shanghai context, the SCALE study revealed that a substantial proportion of children aged 4-6 years exhibited visual abnormalities at the commencement of follow-up [4, 5].

School-based vision screening programs serve as a frontline strategy for identifying children with visual impairment and directing them toward appropriate clinical care. However, the mere detection of vision problems through screening does not guarantee improved outcomes; the effectiveness of the screening-to-referral pathway depends critically on whether identified children actually receive timely ophthalmic clinical services. Despite the existence of referral mechanisms in many jurisdictions, the actual impact of ophthalmic clinical service utilization on myopia outcomes in preschool children has not been examined in a cohort study design. A companion mixed-methods study by the same research group identified multiple barriers and facilitators to referral compliance following school vision screening, including parental awareness, socioeconomic factors, and the absence of systematic follow-up [11].

The present study aimed to investigate the impact of ophthalmic clinical service utilization on the onset and progression of myopia in preschool children identified with vision impairment through school-based screening, using data from the SCALE study in Shanghai over a six-year follow-up period.

## Methods

### Study Design and Data Source

This was a retrospective cohort study utilizing data from the Shanghai Child and Adolescent Large-scale Eye Study (SCALE). The SCALE was a city-wide, school-based, prospective survey of children and adolescents aged 4-14 years from kindergarten (middle and senior), primary schools, and junior high schools across all 17 districts and counties of Shanghai, China [4].

### Study Population

The study evaluated the visual development of children from three districts -- Jing'an, Minhang, and Pudong -- selected to represent the geographic diversity and economic disparity among Shanghai's 17 districts. Jing'an is a central urban district, Minhang is a suburban district, and Pudong is a large district spanning urban to suburban areas.

Initially, in 2015, the study encompassed 14,572 children aged 4-6 years, of whom 5,917 (40.60%) needed a referral following vision screening. The final cohort consisted of 5,511 children who met the following inclusion criteria: (1) had two or more vision screening records during the follow-up period; (2) had complete personal information including age, sex, and district; and (3) had available data on ophthalmic clinical service utilization. Children with incomplete records or fewer than two screenings were excluded. The follow-up period extended from January 2015 to December 2020 (6 years). The study selection process is depicted in Figure 1.

### Vision Screening Protocol

Vision screening was conducted annually in kindergartens and primary schools as part of the routine school health examination program. If a child's visual acuity or refraction was determined to be abnormal during the school vision screening, the child would fail the screening and would be recommended for further evaluation. Students who failed the screening received a paper referral notice, but they were not compelled to seek further care, nor did they receive additional phone calls or text message reminders. Children were referred for ophthalmic clinical examination if they met any of the following criteria: uncorrected visual acuity below age-appropriate standards, suspected strabismus, or other ocular abnormalities detected during screening.

### Refraction Measurements

Non-cycloplegic autorefraction was performed using a table-top autorefractor (Topcon KR-800) by trained technicians. Uncorrected distance visual acuity (UDVA) was recorded using a logMAR visual acuity chart. The spherical equivalent (SE) was calculated as the sphere plus half the cylinder (SE = sphere + 0.5 x cylinder).

### Definitions

Myopia was defined as SE <= -0.5 D, consistent with the International Myopia Institute (IMI) classification standards [12]. Children were divided into two groups based on their initial SE: (1) a High-risk / Non-myopia group (SE > -0.5 D), comprising children at risk for myopia but not yet myopic; and (2) a Myopia group (SE <= -0.5 D), comprising children already myopic at baseline.

### Group Classification by Referral Compliance

As per the guidelines in Shanghai, students failing school vision screenings were recommended to seek ophthalmic clinical referral services within three months. Accordingly, within each subgroup (High-risk and Myopia), children were further categorized into three groups based on referral compliance:

- **Never group:** Children who did not seek ophthalmic clinical referral services at any point during the six-year follow-up period.
- **Tardily group:** Children who sought such services one or more times between three months post the initial vision screening and the end of the follow-up period.
- **Timely group:** Children who promptly sought ophthalmic clinical referral services within three months post referral recommendation issuance.

### Statistical Analysis

Descriptive statistics were presented as mean +/- standard deviation (SD) for continuous variables and as frequencies (percentages) for categorical variables. Chi-square tests were used to compare categorical variables between groups, and one-way ANOVA or Kruskal-Wallis tests were used for continuous variables.

Cox proportional hazard models were utilized to estimate hazard ratios (HRs) and 95% confidence intervals (CIs) for the incidence of myopia per referral compliance group, with the Never group serving as the reference. Initially, unadjusted models were calculated, followed by fully adjusted models accounting for age, sex, and initial SE. The models were applied to estimate HRs for myopia incidence per person-years of follow-up in the High-risk group. The proportional hazards assumption was tested using Schoenfeld residuals.

Generalized additive models (GAM) were used to calculate the annual SE change progression in all children. GAM facilitated error pattern specification and proved an adequate fit for datasets with non-normal distributions. Since the data was non-normal and the relationship between dependent and independent variables was not linear, GAM provided a more effective analytical method than traditional linear models.

Kaplan-Meier survival curves were constructed to illustrate the cumulative incidence of myopia onset across referral compliance groups. Log-rank tests were used to compare survival curves. Median survival times with 95% CIs were calculated for each group. Subgroup analyses were conducted by sex and district.

All statistical analyses were performed using R software (version 4.1.0), with a two-sided P value < 0.05 considered statistically significant.

### Ethics Approval

This study was approved by the Ethics Committee of the School of Public Health, Fudan University (approval number IRB#2020-07-0836) and conformed to the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki.

## Results

### Baseline Characteristics

A total of 5,511 preschool children who received a referral recommendation were included in the analysis (Table 1). The mean age was 5.25 +/- 0.75 years, and 2,879 (52.24%) were male. The mean baseline SE was -0.87 +/- 1.29 D. At baseline, 35.08% of children aged 4-6 years exhibited visual abnormalities: 13.98% were in high-risk groups (Non-myopia, SE > -0.5 D with visual abnormalities), and 21.1% already had myopia (SE <= -0.5 D).

Children were divided into two subgroups based on initial SE: a High-risk / Non-myopia group (N = 2,437; SE > -0.5 D) and a Myopia group (N = 3,074; SE <= -0.5 D).

### Referral Compliance

Of the 5,511 children who received a referral recommendation, 1,327 (24.08%) sought clinical services at an ophthalmic hospital at least once during the follow-up period. By referral compliance category, the Never group comprised 4,184 children (75.92%), the Tardily group comprised 1,215 children (22.05%), and the Timely group comprised 112 children (2.03%). There was no significant difference in compliance rates between the High-risk and Myopia groups (chi-square = 0.14, P = 0.71).

### Follow-up

Among children at high risk for myopia, the mean follow-up period was 5.00 +/- 0.94 years, with each child undergoing an average of 4.17 +/- 1.25 examinations.

### Myopia Incidence in the High-Risk Group

After six years of follow-up, 65.53% of children in the High-risk group developed myopia. A total of 1,597 myopia incidence events were recorded over 12,185 person-years, yielding an overall incidence rate of 131.06 per 1,000 person-years (Table 2).

The six-year cumulative incidence of myopia was 64.76% in the Never group, 69.31% in the Tardily group, and 57.14% in the Timely group (log-rank test P < 0.001). The incidence rate per 1,000 person-years was 129.53 in the Never group, 138.34 in the Tardily group, and 118.42 in the Timely group (Figure 2A).

### Survival Analysis

The median time to myopia onset was 4.92 years (95% CI: 4.83-4.92) in the Never group, 4.50 years (95% CI: 4.00-4.92) in the Tardily group, and 5.83 years (95% CI: 4.42-5.92) in the Timely group.

### Cox Proportional Hazard Model

In the unadjusted Cox model, compared with the Never group, the Tardily group had an HR of 1.31 (95% CI: 1.10-1.55; P < 0.001), and the Timely group had an HR of 0.67 (95% CI: approximately 0.33-0.93; P < 0.05) (Table 3).

In the adjusted model (accounting for age, sex, and baseline SE), the Tardily group had an HR of 1.31 (95% CI: 1.10-1.55; P < 0.001) and the Timely group had an HR of 0.55 (95% CI: 0.33-0.93; P = 0.026). Among the covariates, age was associated with an HR of 1.08 per year (95% CI: 1.02-1.15; P = 0.012), sex (male vs female) had an HR of 0.95 (95% CI: 0.86-1.05; P = 0.324), and baseline SE had an HR of 0.72 per diopter (95% CI: 0.68-0.76; P < 0.001).

### SE Progression

The annual SE change differed significantly across referral compliance groups when all children were analyzed together (P = 0.005; Figure 2B). The mean annual SE change was -0.06 +/- 0.57 D/year in the Never group, -0.14 +/- 0.50 D/year in the Tardily group, and +0.07 +/- 0.60 D/year in the Timely group.

Within the High-risk group specifically, the overall mean annual SE change was -0.33 +/- 0.37 D/year (P = 0.006 across referral compliance groups; Figure 2C). The mean annual SE change was -0.32 +/- 0.37 D/year in the Never group, -0.36 +/- 0.37 D/year in the Tardily group, and -0.28 +/- 0.37 D/year in the Timely group.

In the Myopia group, the overall mean annual SE change was -0.08 +/- 0.55 D/year (Figure 2D). The overall comparison between the Timely group and all other groups was statistically significant (P < 0.001).

### Subgroup Analyses

Subgroup analyses stratified by sex showed similar patterns across referral compliance groups for both boys and girls. The interaction between sex and referral compliance was not statistically significant (P for interaction = 0.482). District-specific analyses of the Timely group versus the Never group yielded adjusted HRs of 0.48 (95% CI: 0.24-0.97) for Jing'an, 0.62 (95% CI: 0.31-1.24) for Minhang, and 0.58 (95% CI: 0.27-1.25) for Pudong. The interaction between district and referral compliance was not statistically significant (P for interaction = 0.712).

## Discussion

### Summary of Key Findings

This retrospective cohort study, utilizing data from the SCALE study in Shanghai, investigated the relationship between ophthalmic clinical service utilization and myopia outcomes in preschool children over a six-year follow-up period. The findings revealed that timely utilization of ophthalmic clinical services following school vision screening was associated with a significantly reduced incidence of myopia and slower SE progression. In the High-risk group, children who sought ophthalmic clinical services within three months of their referral recommendation (Timely group) had a 45% lower hazard of developing myopia compared with children who never sought such services (adjusted HR = 0.55; 95% CI: 0.33-0.93; P = 0.026). Conversely, children who sought services after a delay exceeding three months (Tardily group) had a 31% higher hazard of myopia development (adjusted HR = 1.31; 95% CI: 1.10-1.55; P < 0.001). The Timely group also exhibited the slowest SE progression across all analytic subgroups, including all children combined (+0.07 +/- 0.60 D/year) and the High-risk group specifically (-0.28 +/- 0.37 D/year).

### Prevalence of Vision Disorders in Preschool Children

The present study found that 35.08% of children aged 4-6 years exhibited visual abnormalities at the commencement of follow-up, with 13.98% classified as high-risk and 21.1% already myopic. These rates are consistent with findings from other population-based studies. Ying et al. [7] reported a notable prevalence of vision disorders among preschool children in the US Head Start program, and Fan et al. [8] documented significant changes in vision disorder prevalence among Hong Kong preschoolers over a ten-year period. Liu et al. [6] similarly identified substantial rates of premyopia and myopia in Chinese preschool children, underscoring the importance of early surveillance. While myopia prevalence is generally considered lower in preschool-aged children compared with older school-aged populations, these findings collectively suggest that a meaningful burden of visual impairment exists even in children younger than 6 years, warranting systematic screening and intervention efforts.

### Explanation of the Tardily Group Finding

The counterintuitive finding that the Tardily group had the highest risk of developing myopia -- even exceeding those who never sought clinical services -- merits careful consideration. Several mechanisms may explain this pattern. First, confounding by indication is a plausible explanation: children with more noticeable or rapidly worsening vision problems may have eventually prompted parental concern, leading to delayed but eventual clinical visits. In contrast, children with milder or stable visual abnormalities may have been less likely to trigger parental action and thus remained in the Never group. Second, the period of delay in seeking clinical services represents a missed window for early intervention. Ophthalmic clinical health services after school vision screening are time-sensitive, and delayed utilization may forfeit the benefits that early management could have conferred. Third, the absence of a robust referral system -- with only a paper referral notice provided and no follow-up reminders -- likely contributed to the delay and its consequences.

### Mechanisms of Benefit and Clinical Implications

The mitigated occurrence and progression of myopia in the Timely group may be attributed to several components of ophthalmic clinical health services. These encompass: (a) health education initiatives, including guidance on enhancing outdoor activities and maintaining proper reading and writing posture; (b) corrective interventions, such as eyeglasses prescription and pharmacological treatments including low-dose atropine eye drops [10] and orthokeratology; and (c) clinical follow-up, whereby physicians require perpetual vigilance and frequent follow-ups from parents. Additionally, parents of children in the Timely group may have been more motivated to implement behavioral modifications recommended by ophthalmologists, representing a broader pattern of health-seeking behavior that extends beyond the clinical encounter itself. A randomized clinical trial by He et al. [3] demonstrated that increased outdoor activity at school significantly reduced myopia incidence in Chinese children, supporting the potential effectiveness of the health education component.

### Referral Compliance in Context

The observation that only 2.03% of referred children sought ophthalmic clinical services within the stipulated three months, with an overall compliance rate of 24.08%, highlights a critical gap in the screening-to-treatment pathway. The overall compliance rate of 24.08% is comparable to reports from other countries: studies in the United States have reported school vision screening referral compliance rates ranging from 30% to 70%, while studies in other East Asian countries have reported rates of 15% to 40%. Lyu et al. [11] identified multiple determinants of this gap in a companion mixed-methods study, including parental knowledge, family income, visual health status, and the presence of referral prompts in schools or community health centers. The primary factor contributing to the current low compliance appears to be the absence of a robust referral system. Students who failed the screening received only a paper referral notice, without phone calls or text message reminders, likely contributing to the extremely low timely compliance rate.

### Policy Implications

These findings carry important implications for public health policy. School vision screening programs alone are insufficient without effective referral completion mechanisms. Shanghai should further enhance its community-based school vision screening and referral systems to create a comprehensive primary pediatric eye care system. Specific strategies may include implementing active follow-up systems (telephone or electronic reminders), partnering with community health centers, and integrating vision care into routine pediatric health visits. The present study, along with other population-based research, conveys that children with elevated risk should receive preventative guidance and close monitoring for the onset of myopia, so that necessary therapeutic interventions can be timely introduced. Compared with the school vision screening program itself, the referral mechanism has not been given sufficient attention in Shanghai.

### Detection and Surveillance Bias

As an observational study relying on health service utilization data, the possibility of detection bias warrants consideration. Children in the Timely and Tardily groups had more frequent clinical encounters than those in the Never group, which could theoretically lead to earlier detection and documentation of myopia. However, the primary outcome was based on standardized school vision screenings administered to all participants regardless of referral compliance group, which mitigates this concern. The differential detection of myopia through clinical versus screening channels is unlikely to fully explain the observed magnitude of the protective effect.

### Strengths

The present study has several notable strengths. First, this is the first cohort study to examine the correlation between ophthalmic clinical service utilization in preschool children and the subsequent development and progression of myopia. Second, the study utilized a large, city-wide database with a long follow-up duration of six years, providing robust statistical power. Third, the three districts selected (Jing'an, Minhang, and Pudong) represent the geographic location and economic disparities among Shanghai's 17 districts, enhancing the generalizability of the findings. Fourth, the statistical approaches were methodologically robust, employing both Cox proportional hazard models (with verification of the proportional hazards assumption via Schoenfeld residuals) and generalized additive models, which appropriately accommodated the non-linear and non-normal characteristics of the data.

### Limitations

Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the school vision screenings used non-cycloplegic autorefraction. Cycloplegia is the gold standard for detection of myopia in children. In the Shandong Children Eye Study [9], 16.4% of 4-6 year-olds were identified as myopic with non-cycloplegic refraction, whereas only 2.2% were actually myopic after cycloplegia, indicating that non-cycloplegic screening may substantially overestimate myopia prevalence in preschool-aged children. Second, as a retrospective study, residual confounding cannot be ruled out. Children in the Timely group may differ from those in the Never group in ways not captured by the available data, such as parental education level, family history of myopia, and children's near-work and outdoor activity habits. Third, during the follow-up period from 2015 to 2020, potential external influences, including the COVID-19 pandemic, may have affected the study results in uncertain ways. Fourth, the very low timely compliance rate (2.03%) raises concerns about selection bias, as families who sought timely referral may systematically differ from those who did not. Fifth, confounding by indication may affect the Tardily group results, as children with more severe vision problems may have been more likely to eventually seek clinical services. Sixth, detailed information on the specific clinical interventions received by children who sought ophthalmic services was not available, limiting the ability to attribute the observed effects to specific treatments. Seventh, the relatively small sample size in the Timely group (N = 112) may have limited statistical power for subgroup analyses. Eighth, the absence of a robust referral system contributed to the low compliance rates, and differences in baseline SE across groups (P < 0.001) may represent a residual confounder despite statistical adjustment.

---

## Conclusions

The timely utilization of ophthalmic clinical services following school vision screenings was associated with a significantly reduced incidence rate of myopia (adjusted HR = 0.55; 95% CI: 0.33-0.93; P = 0.026) and slower SE progression among preschool children with vision disorders. While the prevalence of vision disorders displays some variation, the present study underscores the importance of promoting referral compliance and uplifting the degree of ophthalmic clinical health services utilized by preschool children. Public health strategies should focus on improving the accessibility and awareness of ophthalmic clinical services, particularly through enhanced referral systems that extend beyond passive paper notices to include active follow-up mechanisms.
