ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Background:
Although school screenings identify children with vision problems and issue
referrals for medical treatment at an ophthalmic hospital, the effectiveness of
this approach remains unverified. The study aimed to investigate the impact of
ophthalmic clinical services on the onset and progression of myopia in
preschool children identified with vision impairment.

Methods:
Using data from the Shanghai Child and Adolescent Large-scale Eye Study
(SCALE), this retrospective cohort study evaluated the visual development of
children from three districts -- Jing'an, Minhang, and Pudong -- which are
representative of geographic diversity and economic disparity in Shanghai's 17
districts. Initially, in 2015, the study encompassed 14,572 children aged 4-6
years, of whom 5,917 needed a referral. The cohort consisted of 5,511 children
who had two or more vision screenings and complete personal information over
the follow-up period from January 2015 to December 2020. Within each subgroup,
children were further categorized into Never, Tardily, and Timely groups based
on their referral compliance to compare the differences in the occurrence and
progression of myopia. Cox proportional models were applied to estimate hazard
ratios (HRs) for myopia incidence per person-years of follow-up in the
High-risk group. Generalized additive models (GAM) were used to calculate the
progression for annual spherical equivalent changes in all children.

Results:
Of the 5,511 preschool children (mean age, 5.25 years; 52.24% male) who
received a referral recommendation, 1,327 (24.08%) sought clinical services at
an ophthalmic hospital. After six years of follow-up, 65.53% of children
developed myopia. The six-year cumulative incidence of myopia in the Never,
Tardily, and Timely groups was 64.76%, 69.31%, and 57.14%, respectively.

Conclusions:
The timely utilization of ophthalmic clinical services following school vision
screenings demonstrated a significantly reduced incidence rate of both myopia
and spherical equivalent (SE) progression among preschool children with vision
disorders. While the prevalence of vision disorders displays some variation,
this study underscores the importance of uplifting the degree of ophthalmic
clinical health services utilized by preschool children.

================================================================================
BACKGROUND
================================================================================

Myopia is commonly seen among school-aged children, whereas its prevalence is
relatively low in children aged 6 years or younger. However, congruent with
other longitudinal studies, the authors identified a higher rate of vision
disorders among preschool children.

In this study, 35.08% of children aged between 4 and 6 years exhibited visual
abnormalities at the commencement of the follow-up period. Further, 13.98%
were in high-risk groups and 21.1% already had myopia.

Although school screenings identify children with vision problems and issue
referrals for medical treatment at an ophthalmic hospital, the effectiveness of
this approach remains unverified. No other cohort studies have examined the
correlation between ophthalmic clinical service utilization in preschool
children and the subsequent development and progression of myopia.

The study aimed to investigate the impact of ophthalmic clinical services on
the onset and progression of myopia in preschool children identified with
vision impairment.

================================================================================
METHODS
================================================================================

Study Design and Data Source:
-----------------------------
This was a retrospective cohort study using data from the Shanghai Child and
Adolescent Large-scale Eye Study (SCALE). The SCALE was a city-wide,
school-based, prospective survey. Children and adolescents aged 4-14 years from
kindergarten (middle and senior), primary schools, and junior high schools of
all 17 districts and counties of the city of Shanghai, China were examined.

Study Population:
-----------------
The study evaluated the visual development of children from three districts --
Jing'an, Minhang, and Pudong -- which are representative of geographic
diversity and economic disparity in Shanghai's 17 districts. Jing'an is a
central urban district, Minhang is a suburban district, and Pudong is a large
district spanning urban to suburban areas.

Initially, in 2015, the study encompassed 14,572 children aged 4-6 years, of
whom 5,917 (40.60%) needed a referral. The cohort consisted of 5,511 children
who met the following inclusion criteria:
  (1) Had two or more vision screening records during the follow-up period
  (2) Had complete personal information including age, sex, and district
  (3) Had available data on ophthalmic clinical service utilization

Children with incomplete records or fewer than two screenings were excluded.
Follow-up period: January 2015 to December 2020 (6 years).

Figure 1 presents the flowchart for selecting the study subjects.

Vision Screening Protocol:
--------------------------
Vision screening was conducted annually in kindergartens and primary schools as
part of the routine school health examination program.

If a child's visual acuity or refraction was determined to be abnormal during
the school vision screening, the child would fail the screening and would be
recommended for further evaluation. Students who failed the screening would
receive a paper referral notice, but they were not compelled to do so, nor did
they receive further phone calls or text message reminders.

Children were referred for ophthalmic clinical examination if they met any of
the following criteria: uncorrected visual acuity below age-appropriate
standards, suspected strabismus, or other ocular abnormalities detected during
screening.

Refraction Measurements:
------------------------
Non-cycloplegic autorefraction was performed using a table-top autorefractor
(Topcon KR-800) by trained technicians. Uncorrected distance visual acuity
(UDVA) was recorded using a logMAR visual acuity chart. The spherical
equivalent (SE) was calculated as the sphere plus half the cylinder
(SE = sphere + 0.5 x cylinder).

Definitions:
------------
Myopia was defined as spherical equivalent (SE) <= -0.5 D.

Children were divided into two groups based on their initial spherical
equivalent (SE):
  - High-risk / Non-myopia group: SE > -0.5 D (children at risk for myopia
    but not yet myopic)
  - Myopia group: SE <= -0.5 D (children already myopic at baseline)

Group Classification by Referral Compliance:
--------------------------------------------
As per the guidelines in Shanghai, students failing school vision screenings
are recommended to seek ophthalmic clinical referral services within three
months. Accordingly, within each subgroup (High-risk and Myopia), children were
further categorized into three groups based on referral compliance:

  - Never group: Children who did not seek ophthalmic clinical referral
    services at any point during the six-year follow-up period.
  - Tardily group: Children who sought such services one or more times between
    three months post the initial vision screening and the end of the follow-up
    period.
  - Timely group: Children who promptly sought ophthalmic clinical referral
    services within three months post referral recommendation issuance.

Statistical Analysis:
---------------------
Descriptive statistics were presented as mean +/- standard deviation (SD) for
continuous variables and as frequencies (percentages) for categorical variables.
Chi-square tests were used to compare categorical variables between groups, and
one-way ANOVA or Kruskal-Wallis tests were used for continuous variables.

1. Cox Proportional Hazard Models:
   - Cox proportional hazard models were utilized to estimate hazard ratios
     (HRs) and 95% confidence intervals (CIs) for the incidence of myopia per
     referral compliance group, with non-referred children (Never group)
     serving as the reference.
   - Initially, unadjusted models were calculated, followed by fully adjusted
     models accounting for age, sex, and initial SE.
   - Applied to estimate HRs for myopia incidence per person-years of
     follow-up in the High-risk group.
   - The proportional hazards assumption was tested using Schoenfeld residuals.

2. Generalized Additive Models (GAM):
   - Used to calculate the progression for annual spherical equivalent changes
     in all children.
   - The mean rate of change in SE was compared among children with different
     initial age, sex, and SE.
   - The utilization of GAM facilitated error pattern specification and proved
     an adequate fit for datasets with non-normal distributions, yielding lower
     and more reliable p-values.
   - Since the data was non-normal and the relationship between dependent and
     independent variables was not linear, GAM provided a more effective
     analytical method than traditional linear models.
   - The annual SE change was calculated as the difference in SE between
     consecutive screenings divided by the time interval.

3. Additional analyses:
   - Kaplan-Meier survival curves were constructed to illustrate the cumulative
     incidence of myopia onset across referral compliance groups.
   - Log-rank tests were used to compare survival curves.
   - Median survival times with 95% CIs were calculated for each group.
   - Subgroup analyses were conducted by sex and district.

All statistical analyses were performed using R software (version 4.1.0), with
a two-sided P value < 0.05 considered statistically significant.

DISCUSSION
================================================================================

This retrospective cohort study, using data from the SCALE study in Shanghai,
demonstrated that timely utilization of ophthalmic clinical services following
school vision screening was associated with a significantly reduced incidence
and slower progression of myopia in preschool children over a 6-year follow-up
period.

Key Discussion Points:

1. PREVALENCE OF VISION DISORDERS IN PRESCHOOL CHILDREN:
   - 35.08% of children aged 4-6 years exhibited visual abnormalities at the
     commencement of the follow-up period.
   - 13.98% were in high-risk groups and 21.1% already had myopia.
   - Myopia is commonly seen among school-aged children, whereas its prevalence
     is relatively low in children aged 6 years or younger. However, congruent
     with other longitudinal studies, a higher rate of vision disorders was
     identified among preschool children.

2. EFFECTIVENESS OF TIMELY REFERRAL:
   - The finding that the Timely group had a 45% lower risk of developing
     myopia compared to the Never group (HR 0.55; 95% CI: 0.33-0.93)
     underscores the importance of prompt follow-up after vision screening.
   - The mitigated occurrence and progression of myopia in the Timely referred
     group was attributed to their timely utilization of ophthalmic health
     services, which encompass:
     a) Health education initiatives: enhancing outdoor activities and
        maintaining proper reading and writing posture.
     b) Corrective interventions: eyeglasses prescription and medication usage
        (e.g., low-dose atropine eye drops, orthokeratology).
     c) Clinical follow-up: physicians often require perpetual vigilance and
        frequent follow-ups from parents.
   - Parents of children in the Timely group may have been more motivated to
     implement behavioral modifications recommended by ophthalmologists.

3. TARDILY GROUP HAD HIGHEST RISK:
   - Children who were referred for eye examinations at preschool, but did so
     tardily, presented the highest risk for developing myopia -- even
     outstripping those who never sought a referral (HR 1.31; 95% CI:
     1.10-1.55; P < 0.001).
   - This counterintuitive finding may be explained by:
     a) Confounding by indication: children with more noticeable or rapidly
        worsening vision problems may have eventually sought care (tardily),
        while milder cases never did.
     b) The period of delay in seeking clinical services represented a missed
        window for early intervention.

4. LOW REFERRAL COMPLIANCE:
   - Only a mere 2.03% of children required to seek ophthalmic clinical
     services did so within the stipulated three months.
   - Overall compliance was 24.08% (any visit during follow-up).
   - The overall compliance rate of 24.08% is comparable to reports from other
     countries. Studies in the United States have reported school vision
     screening referral compliance rates ranging from 30% to 70%, while studies
     in other East Asian countries have reported rates of 15% to 40%.
   - The primary factor contributing to the current low compliance is the
     absence of a robust referral system.
   - Student age, visual health status, family annual income, and the presence
     of referral prompts in schools or community health centers all impact the
     utilization of ophthalmic clinical health services.

5. POLICY IMPLICATIONS:
   - This study, along with several other population-based researches, conveys
     that children with elevated risk should receive preventative guidance and
     close monitoring for the onset of myopia, so that necessary therapeutic
     interventions can be timely introduced.
   - Compared to the school vision screening program, the referral mechanism
     has not been given sufficient attention in Shanghai.
   - This necessitates the implementation of efficacious school screening
     programs and expedited referrals for optical correction and myopia
     management.
   - Shanghai should further enhance its community-based school vision
     screening and referral systems to create a comprehensive primary pediatric
     eye care system.
   - Ophthalmic clinical health services after school vision screening are
     time-sensitive.

Strengths:
----------
  - Large, city-wide database with long-term follow-up (6 years).
  - No other cohort studies have examined the correlation between ophthalmic
    clinical service utilization in preschool children and the subsequent
    development and progression of myopia.
  - The three districts selected (Jing'an, Minhang, Pudong) represent the
    geographic location and economic disparities amongst Shanghai's 17
    districts, enhancing generalizability.
  - Robust statistical approaches: Cox proportional hazard models and
    generalized additive models.
  - The use of GAM allowed appropriate modeling of nonlinear trajectories.

Limitations:
------------
  1. Non-cycloplegic refraction: The school vision screenings used
     non-cycloplegic methods. Cycloplegia is the gold standard for detection of
     myopia in children. In the Shandong Children Eye Study, while 16.4% of
     4-6 year-olds were identified as myopic with non-cycloplegic refraction
     (NCR), only 2.2% were actually myopic after cycloplegia. This indicates
     that non-cycloplegic screening may substantially overestimate myopia
     prevalence in preschool-aged children.

  2. Retrospective design and residual confounding: This was a retrospective
     study, and residual confounding cannot be ruled out. Children in the Timely
     group may differ from those in the Never group in ways not captured by the
     available data, such as parental education level, family history of myopia,
     and children's near-work and outdoor activity habits.

  3. Potential confounding from unknown influences: During the follow-up period
     from 2015 to 2020, there might have been unknown influences (such as the
     COVID-19 pandemic) affecting the study results, which remained uncertain.

  4. Selection bias / Low referral compliance: Only 2.03% of children sought
     services within three months. This very low compliance rate raises concerns
     about selection bias, as families who did seek timely referral may
     systematically differ from those who did not (e.g., socioeconomic status,
     parental awareness, severity of vision problems).

  5. Confounding by indication: Children in the Tardily group may have had more
     severe or noticeable vision problems prompting eventual (but delayed)
     clinical visits, which could confound the association between timing of
     referral and myopia outcomes.

  6. Lack of intervention detail: Detailed information on the specific clinical
     interventions received by children who sought ophthalmic services was not
     available, limiting the ability to attribute the observed effects to
     specific treatments.

  7. Small Timely group sample size: The relatively small sample size in the
     Timely group (N = 112) may have limited statistical power for subgroup
     analyses.

  8. Absence of a robust referral system contributed to low compliance rates.

================================================================================
CONCLUSIONS
================================================================================

The timely utilization of ophthalmic clinical services following school vision
screenings demonstrated a significantly reduced incidence rate of both myopia
and spherical equivalent (SE) progression among preschool children with vision
disorders.

Timely utilization of ophthalmic clinical services among children aged 4 to 6
years who fail school vision screenings can significantly reduce the incidence
of myopia and slow SE progression.

While the prevalence of vision disorders displays some variation, this study
underscores the importance of uplifting the degree of ophthalmic clinical
health services utilized by preschool children.

These findings underscore the importance of promoting referral compliance after
school vision screening, particularly among preschool-aged children, to enable
early intervention and potentially reduce the burden of myopia. Public health
strategies should focus on improving the accessibility and awareness of
ophthalmic clinical services, particularly in districts with lower referral
compliance rates.
