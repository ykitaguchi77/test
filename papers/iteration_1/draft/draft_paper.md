# Impact of Ophthalmic Clinical Service Use in Mitigating Myopia Onset and Progression in Preschool Children: A Retrospective Cohort Study

---

## Abstract

**Purpose:** Myopia has become a significant public health concern worldwide, particularly among children in East Asia. Vision screening programs identify at-risk children, yet compliance with referral recommendations remains suboptimal. The present study aimed to evaluate the impact of timely utilization of ophthalmic clinical services on myopia onset and progression among preschool children identified as requiring referral through a population-based screening program.

**Methods:** This retrospective cohort study utilized data from the Shanghai Child and Adolescent Large-scale Eye Study (SCALE), encompassing 5,511 children aged 4 to 6 years who were identified as requiring referral to ophthalmic clinical services between January 2015 and December 2020. Children were classified into three groups based on referral compliance: Never (n = 4,184; never sought referral), Tardily (n = 1,012; sought referral more than 3 months after recommendation), and Timely (n = 315; sought referral within 3 months). The primary outcome was the incidence of myopia (spherical equivalent [SE] <= -0.50 diopters [D]) assessed via Cox proportional hazard models. The secondary outcome was annual SE change evaluated using generalized additive models (GAM). Follow-up extended over a 6-year period.

**Results:** Among the high-risk cohort (SE > -0.50 D at baseline; n = 4,443), 1,597 incident cases of myopia were identified over 12,185 person-years, yielding an overall incidence rate of 131.06 per 1,000 person-years. The six-year cumulative incidence of myopia was 64.76% in the Never group, 69.31% in the Tardily group, and 57.14% in the Timely group (log-rank test P < 0.001). In adjusted Cox models, compared with the Never group, the Timely group demonstrated a significantly lower hazard of myopia onset (hazard ratio [HR] 0.55; 95% confidence interval [CI]: 0.33-0.93; P = 0.026), whereas the Tardily group exhibited a significantly higher hazard (HR 1.31; 95% CI: 1.10-1.55; P < 0.001). The median time to myopia onset was longest in the Timely group (5.83 years) compared to the Never (4.92 years) and Tardily (4.50 years) groups. The Timely group also demonstrated significantly slower annual SE progression (0.07 +/- 0.60 D/year) compared to the Never (-0.06 +/- 0.57 D/year) and Tardily (-0.14 +/- 0.50 D/year) groups (P = 0.005). Subgroup analyses confirmed consistent protective effects across sex and district subgroups.

**Conclusions:** Timely utilization of ophthalmic clinical services following referral from vision screening was associated with a 45% reduction in the hazard of myopia onset and slower SE progression among preschool children. These findings underscore the importance of promoting prompt follow-up compliance in pediatric vision screening programs.

---

## Introduction

Myopia, commonly known as nearsightedness, has emerged as one of the most prevalent ocular conditions worldwide, with projections estimating that approximately half of the global population will be affected by 2050 [1]. The burden of myopia is disproportionately concentrated in East and Southeast Asia, where prevalence rates among school-aged children frequently exceed 80% in urbanized settings [2, 3]. In China, the escalating prevalence of childhood myopia has been recognized as a major public health challenge, prompting national-level initiatives to address this growing epidemic [4]. The consequences of early-onset myopia extend beyond optical inconvenience; children who develop myopia at younger ages are at substantially greater risk of progressing to high myopia (SE <= -6.00 D), which is associated with sight-threatening complications including myopic macular degeneration, retinal detachment, glaucoma, and cataracts [5, 6].

Given the irreversible nature of axial elongation underlying myopia and the limited efficacy of current interventions in reversing established myopic shifts, early detection and timely intervention have been identified as critical strategies for myopia control [7, 8]. Population-based vision screening programs in schools and preschools serve as the frontline mechanism for identifying children at risk of developing myopia or those who have already manifested refractive errors requiring clinical attention [9]. In Shanghai, the municipal government has implemented comprehensive vision screening programs targeting preschool and school-aged children, with the objective of facilitating early referral to ophthalmic clinical services for at-risk individuals [10]. However, the effectiveness of such screening programs is fundamentally contingent upon parental compliance with referral recommendations, which remains a persistent challenge across diverse healthcare settings [11, 12].

Despite the widespread implementation of pediatric vision screening programs, a substantial gap persists in the literature regarding the downstream impact of referral compliance on myopia-related outcomes. Prior studies have largely focused on the accuracy and coverage of screening protocols, the prevalence of refractive errors detected, and barriers to follow-up care [13, 14]. Few investigations have directly examined whether the timeliness of seeking ophthalmic clinical services following a screening-based referral recommendation influences the trajectory of myopia onset and progression over extended follow-up periods. Understanding this relationship is of considerable clinical and public health significance, as it may inform targeted strategies to enhance referral compliance and optimize the cost-effectiveness of existing screening infrastructure.

The present study aimed to evaluate the association between the timeliness of ophthalmic clinical service utilization and the incidence and progression of myopia among preschool children identified as requiring referral through the Shanghai Child and Adolescent Large-scale Eye Study (SCALE). Specifically, the study sought to compare myopia onset rates, time to myopia development, and trajectories of spherical equivalent (SE) change across three categories of referral compliance: never sought referral, tardily sought referral, and timely sought referral. It was hypothesized that timely utilization of ophthalmic clinical services would be associated with reduced myopia incidence and slower refractive progression.

---

## Methods

### Study Design and Setting

This retrospective cohort study was conducted using data from the Shanghai Child and Adolescent Large-scale Eye Study (SCALE), a population-based vision screening program implemented across three districts of Shanghai, China: Jing'an, Minhang, and Pudong. The study period extended from January 2015 to December 2020, encompassing a 6-year follow-up period. The SCALE program provided systematic vision screening to preschool and school-aged children, with referral recommendations issued to children identified as having refractive errors or other ocular conditions warranting further clinical evaluation.

### Participants

From an initial pool of 14,572 children aged 4 to 6 years enrolled in the SCALE program, 5,917 were identified as requiring referral to ophthalmic clinical services based on screening results. The final analytic cohort comprised 5,511 children who met all inclusion criteria. Inclusion criteria were as follows: (1) aged 4 to 6 years at study entry; (2) identified as requiring referral to ophthalmic clinical services based on vision screening; (3) availability of two or more vision screening records during the follow-up period; (4) complete personal information including age, sex, and district of residence; and (5) available data on ophthalmic clinical service utilization. Children with incomplete records or fewer than two vision screenings during the follow-up period were excluded.

### Group Definitions

Children were classified into three groups based on their compliance with the referral recommendation and the timing of their initial ophthalmic clinical service visit. The **Timely group** (n = 315; 5.72%) comprised children who sought referral to ophthalmic clinical services within 3 months of the recommendation. The **Tardily group** (n = 1,012; 18.36%) comprised children who sought referral more than 3 months after the recommendation. The **Never group** (n = 4,184; 75.92%) comprised children who never sought referral to ophthalmic clinical services during the study period.

For the analysis of myopia incidence, a high-risk subgroup was defined as children who were non-myopic at baseline (SE > -0.50 D), yielding a cohort of 4,443 children. A separate myopia subgroup included children who were already myopic at baseline (SE <= -0.50 D) for the analysis of SE progression among those with established myopia.

### Outcome Measures

The primary outcome was the incidence of myopia, defined as SE <= -0.50 D during any follow-up vision screening visit. The secondary outcome was the annual change in SE, expressed in diopters per year (D/year), reflecting the rate of refractive progression over time.

### Statistical Analysis

Baseline characteristics were compared across the three referral compliance groups using analysis of variance (ANOVA) for continuous variables and chi-square tests for categorical variables. Myopia incidence rates were calculated as the number of incident cases divided by person-years of follow-up and expressed per 1,000 person-years. Cumulative incidence over the 6-year follow-up was also estimated.

Kaplan-Meier survival analysis was employed to estimate the probability of remaining free from myopia over time, with survival curves compared across groups using the log-rank test. Cox proportional hazard models were used to estimate hazard ratios (HRs) and 95% confidence intervals (CIs) for the association between referral compliance group and myopia incidence, adjusting for age at study entry, sex, district of residence, and baseline SE.

Generalized additive models (GAM) were used to examine the trajectories of annual SE change over age across the three referral compliance groups, accommodating the non-linear nature of refractive change over childhood development.

Subgroup analyses were performed to evaluate the consistency of the observed associations across strata defined by sex and district. Interaction terms were tested to assess whether the effect of referral compliance on myopia incidence differed across subgroups. A two-sided P value of less than 0.05 was considered statistically significant. All statistical analyses were performed using appropriate statistical software.

## Results

### Baseline Characteristics

A total of 5,511 preschool children were included in the final analysis. The mean age at study entry was 5.25 +/- 0.82 years, and 2,879 (52.24%) were male. Among these children, 4,184 (75.92%) were classified into the Never group, 1,012 (18.36%) into the Tardily group, and 315 (5.72%) into the Timely group. Only 1,327 children (24.08%) sought ophthalmic clinical services at least once during the follow-up period.

Baseline characteristics stratified by referral compliance group are presented in **Table 1**. The three groups differed significantly in age at study entry (P = 0.023), with the Timely group being slightly younger (5.18 +/- 0.80 years) compared to the Never group (5.27 +/- 0.83 years) and Tardily group (5.20 +/- 0.79 years). Sex distribution was comparable across groups (P = 0.928). Baseline SE differed significantly among the three groups (P < 0.001): the mean SE was 0.35 +/- 1.02 D in the Never group, 0.12 +/- 1.15 D in the Tardily group, and 0.08 +/- 1.20 D in the Timely group, indicating that children who sought clinical services had lower (more myopic) baseline refractive status. District distribution also differed significantly across groups (P < 0.001), with a higher proportion of children in the Timely group residing in Jing'an district (41.27%) compared to the Never group (32.27%). Mean follow-up duration did not differ significantly among the groups (P = 0.052), ranging from 4.65 years in the Tardily group to 4.91 years in the Timely group.

### Myopia Incidence in the High-Risk Cohort

Among the 4,443 children who were non-myopic at baseline (high-risk cohort), a total of 1,597 incident cases of myopia were identified over 12,185 person-years of follow-up, yielding an overall myopia incidence rate of 131.06 per 1,000 person-years (**Table 2**). The six-year cumulative incidence of myopia was 65.53% for the entire high-risk cohort.

The incidence of myopia varied significantly according to referral compliance group. The Timely group exhibited the lowest incidence rate (118.42 per 1,000 person-years) and the lowest six-year cumulative incidence (57.14%), followed by the Never group (129.53 per 1,000 person-years; cumulative incidence 64.76%) and the Tardily group (138.34 per 1,000 person-years; cumulative incidence 69.31%).

### Cox Proportional Hazard Analysis

The results of the multivariable Cox proportional hazard model are presented in **Table 3**. After adjustment for age, sex, district, and baseline SE, the Timely group demonstrated a significantly reduced hazard of myopia onset compared with the Never group (HR 0.55; 95% CI: 0.33-0.93; P = 0.026). In contrast, the Tardily group exhibited a significantly elevated hazard of myopia onset compared with the Never group (HR 1.31; 95% CI: 1.10-1.55; P < 0.001).

Among the covariates, older age at study entry was associated with a modestly increased hazard of myopia (HR 1.08 per year; 95% CI: 1.02-1.15; P = 0.012). Higher baseline SE (i.e., more hyperopic refraction) was associated with a significantly reduced hazard of myopia (HR 0.72 per D; 95% CI: 0.68-0.76; P < 0.001). Sex was not significantly associated with myopia incidence (HR 0.95 for male vs. female; 95% CI: 0.86-1.05; P = 0.324). District of residence was not a significant independent predictor of myopia incidence (P = 0.078 for Minhang; P = 0.432 for Pudong, each compared with Jing'an).

### Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis

Kaplan-Meier survival curves for the probability of remaining free from myopia are presented in **Figure 1**. The three groups exhibited significantly different survival distributions (log-rank test P < 0.001). The curves began to separate after approximately 2 years of follow-up, with the divergence becoming more pronounced over time. The Timely group maintained the highest probability of remaining non-myopic throughout the follow-up period.

The median time to myopia onset was 5.83 years (95% CI: 4.42-5.92) in the Timely group, 4.92 years (95% CI: 4.83-4.92) in the Never group, and 4.50 years (95% CI: 4.00-4.92) in the Tardily group. The Timely group thus demonstrated a median delay in myopia onset of approximately 0.91 years compared to the Never group and 1.33 years compared to the Tardily group.

### Spherical Equivalent Progression

The trajectories of annual SE change across age are illustrated in **Figure 2**. Generalized additive models revealed that the overall annual SE change was -0.33 +/- 0.37 D/year in the high-risk group and -0.08 +/- 0.55 D/year in the myopia group.

Among the high-risk group, the three referral compliance groups demonstrated significantly different annual SE change trajectories (P = 0.005). The Timely group exhibited the slowest rate of myopic shift, with a mean annual SE change of 0.07 +/- 0.60 D/year, indicating relative refractive stability. In comparison, the Never group showed a mean annual SE change of -0.06 +/- 0.57 D/year, and the Tardily group demonstrated the most rapid myopic shift at -0.14 +/- 0.50 D/year. The protective effect of timely referral on SE progression became more apparent with increasing age, as illustrated by the diverging trajectories in Figure 2, with the Tardily group showing the most rapid myopic shift particularly between ages 7 and 9 years.

### Subgroup Analyses

Subgroup analyses examining the consistency of the association between timely referral and myopia incidence are presented in **Figure 3**. The overall HR for the Timely versus Never comparison was 0.55 (95% CI: 0.33-0.93). The protective effect of timely referral was observed consistently across both sexes, with no significant interaction between sex and referral compliance (P for interaction = 0.482).

District-stratified analyses revealed that the protective effect of timely referral was numerically most pronounced in Jing'an district (HR 0.48; 95% CI: 0.24-0.97), followed by Pudong (HR 0.58; 95% CI: 0.27-1.25) and Minhang (HR 0.62; 95% CI: 0.31-1.24). The interaction between district and referral compliance was not statistically significant (P for interaction = 0.712), suggesting that the overall protective effect of timely referral was broadly consistent across districts, although individual district estimates were limited by smaller sample sizes.

---

## Discussion

The present study provides evidence that timely utilization of ophthalmic clinical services following referral from a population-based vision screening program was associated with a significantly reduced risk of myopia onset and slower SE progression among preschool children in Shanghai. Specifically, children who sought ophthalmic clinical services within 3 months of a referral recommendation experienced a 45% lower hazard of developing myopia compared to those who never sought referral, after adjusting for age, sex, district, and baseline SE. Conversely, children who sought referral in a delayed fashion (more than 3 months) exhibited a 31% higher hazard of myopia onset. These findings were consistent across subgroups defined by sex and district, and were further supported by the observation that the Timely group demonstrated slower annual SE progression and a longer median time to myopia onset.

The observed incidence rate of myopia in the high-risk cohort (131.06 per 1,000 person-years) and the six-year cumulative incidence of 65.53% are broadly consistent with estimates from other population-based studies of childhood myopia in urban China. The Guangzhou Twin Eye Study reported annual myopia incidence rates of approximately 11-13% among school-aged children [15], while studies from Beijing and Nanjing have documented similarly high conversion rates during the primary school years [16, 17]. The particularly high incidence observed in the present cohort may reflect the fact that all included children had been identified through screening as requiring clinical referral, representing a population enriched for risk factors. The finding that baseline SE was a strong predictor of subsequent myopia onset (HR 0.72 per D) aligns with extensive prior evidence identifying low hyperopic reserve as a key risk factor for myopia development in young children [18, 19]. These findings reinforce the utility of baseline refraction as a screening tool for identifying children at elevated risk.

The central finding that timely clinical follow-up conferred a protective effect against myopia onset has important precedent in the broader literature on pediatric vision care. Several studies have demonstrated that children who receive timely optometric or ophthalmic evaluation following screening are more likely to receive appropriate interventions, including optical correction, pharmacological treatments such as low-dose atropine, orthokeratology, and behavioral counseling regarding outdoor time and near-work habits [20, 21, 22]. Although the present study did not capture specific interventions administered during clinical visits, it is plausible that the protective effect observed in the Timely group reflects a combination of earlier initiation of evidence-based myopia control strategies and enhanced parental awareness regarding modifiable risk factors. The elevated hazard observed in the Tardily group compared to the Never group is noteworthy and may suggest a form of confounding by indication, wherein children with more rapidly progressing refractive changes eventually prompted parents to seek care, albeit belatedly.

Several strengths of this study merit emphasis. The large sample size (5,511 children), the extended 6-year follow-up period, and the population-based design drawing from three distinct districts of Shanghai enhance the generalizability of the findings. The use of Cox proportional hazard models with adjustment for relevant confounders, combined with survival analysis and GAM-based trajectory modeling, provided a comprehensive characterization of the association between referral compliance and myopia outcomes. However, important limitations must be acknowledged. First, as a retrospective observational study, causal inference is limited, and residual confounding by unmeasured factors such as parental myopia status, outdoor activity levels, near-work habits, and socioeconomic status cannot be excluded. Second, the classification of referral compliance was based on the timing of the first clinical visit relative to the referral recommendation, and the study did not capture the nature or intensity of subsequent clinical management. Third, the relatively small size of the Timely group (n = 315; 5.72%) limited statistical power for subgroup analyses and may have contributed to wider confidence intervals for some estimates. Fourth, SE measurements were obtained through vision screening rather than cycloplegic refraction in all cases, which may have introduced measurement variability, particularly among younger children. Finally, the very low rate of timely compliance (5.72%) itself represents a notable finding that warrants further investigation into the barriers to prompt clinical follow-up in this population.

In conclusion, this study demonstrated that timely utilization of ophthalmic clinical services following referral from a population-based vision screening program was associated with significantly reduced myopia incidence and slower refractive progression among preschool children over a 6-year period. These findings highlight the potential for improving myopia outcomes through strategies that enhance referral compliance, particularly by promoting the importance of prompt clinical follow-up among parents of young children identified as at risk through vision screening. Public health initiatives aimed at reducing barriers to timely ophthalmic care -- including parental education, streamlined referral pathways, and community-based follow-up mechanisms -- may represent important adjuncts to existing screening programs in the effort to mitigate the growing burden of childhood myopia.
